Introduction: Tour Guide Tips

Joint Anatomy for Athletic Training builds on Trail Guide to the Body, continuing your journey of discovery. Through actual clinical stories, detailed illustrations, video and photo examples, historical perspectives, and interactive elements (plus the occasional pun), this resource will help you better comprehend the anatomy of joints and their related structures.

In addition to boosting your anatomy knowledge, the information you will find here offers a personalized perspective on why you need a deeper understanding of that anatomy. As a future clinician, you will not merely be treating an anatomical structure—you will be treating a person (1.1).

 

1.1 Clinicians treat the whole person, not just an anatomical structure.

 

Before we get started, here are some tour guide tips to help you on your way.

How to Use This Text

This clinical companion has seven chapters: this introduction plus six chapters that correspond to the body regions in Trail Guide to the Body. To accompany and expand on the Trail Guide chapters, the focus here turns primarily to commonly injured joints (articulations)—their functional anatomy, supporting connective and soft tissue, and bursae, plus a selection of clinically relevant examples to help illustrate the anatomy.

The eTextbook walks you through these areas using stories, descriptions, plentiful figures, and several types of features that will lend you a helping hand (1.2).

 

1.2 This text lends you a helping hand in learning joint anatomy.


Chapter-Opening Stories and Questions

Each chapter opens with a story based on actual events to introduce the topics of the chapter (1.3). You will get an immediate glimpse at how the knowledge of anatomy applies in clinical scenarios.

Chapter-opening questions encourage you to make your own hypotheses based on what you already know. As you read the chapter, keep these hypotheses in mind and refine them as you learn. The questions are revisited at the end of the chapter.

1.3 Chapter-opening story and questions

 

Hyperlinked Definitions and Key Concepts

As you navigate through the text, you will notice that certain words are highlighted and linked to their definitions. These terms may be ones that you do not immediately recall or are not familiar with at that point in the text. Linking to the terms is a tool to assist you in your learning process (and save you some time).

At the beginning of each chapter, you will find a selection of these terms in a Key Concepts list (1.4). These lists point out terms and items that are likely to play a larger role in the chapter.

1.4 Hyperlinked Key Concepts and definitions


Questions

Each chapter has a preview of its content, and Questions features appear at the end of the preview (1.5). These questions prompt you to think about topics you will encounter as you read or applications you may wish to explore further on your own. Which vertebrae are designed for greater mobility? What sports might impact the hip more than others? Why are inversion sprains more common than eversion sprains? Use the questions as guideposts and follow your curiosity.

Connecting the Dots

Connecting the Dots features encourage you to think of clinical examples of the concepts you have just read about (1.6). A few examples are provided to get the juices flowing. As you apply the ideas to your own examples, you will deepen your understanding and increase your retention of the content.

What’s in a Name?

For quick histories of certain names and terms, look for the pop-up feature What’s in a Name? Learning the story behind a name—such as anatomical snuffbox or Lisfranc joint—can assist you in recalling the location and clinical relevance of a structure, condition, or procedure (1.7).

Brain Builders

Brain Builder Quizzes are interactive features appearing throughout the chapters that you can use to review what you have learned in a particular section of text (1.8). Using these quizzes will help you identify what you know and what material you may need to revisit.

Linking It All Together

This end-of-chapter section revisits the questions from the chapter-opening story (1.9). It provides sample answers and links to other research, to inspire discussion with your peers and instructor.

1.9 Linking It All Together 


Learning Objectives

One more important piece of preparation before you go: let us establish the goals that this eTextbook shares with you (1.10).

 

1.10 Embracing a shared purpose

 

This clinical companion to Trail Guide to the Body will help you develop your knowledge so you will be able to:

  1. Name and locate the joint structures that are featured in this eTextbook.
  2. Identify bones, supporting tissue, and bursae of the featured joint structures and complexes.
  3. Describe the functional anatomy of the featured joint structures.
  4. Explain the clinical relevance of the featured joint structures.
  5. Differentiate among the featured joint structures.
  6. Name and describe the cranial nerves and summarize ways to identify their functions.

 

Use these objectives as your guiding principles as you read and study each chapter. Now you are ready to move ahead on your journey into clinically relevant anatomy!
