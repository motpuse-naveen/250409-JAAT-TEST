The Pelvis

The pelvic girdle consists of three stable and virtually nonmoving bones: the pubis, ilium, and ischium (6.24). Think of these bones as connectors. They connect to both the appendicular and axial skeletons through articulations with the femur and sacrum, respectively (6.25). We have already discussed the acetabulofemoral joint, so here we focus on the structure and function of the pelvis.

 

6.24 The bones of the pelvis

 

6.25 A 3D view of the pelvis

 

Most likely, as you are reading this, you are in a seated position. You may not notice it, but the forces from your body weight are actually being distributed through your sacrum and unto your ischial tuberosities. This is even more the case when seated in a neutral pelvis position. By maintaining this position while you are sitting in a chair reading or in class, you have achieved a big accomplishment—a core workout!

The pelvis also plays a prominent role in our daily activities such as walking—and pretty much everything we do. Just think of all the muscles that originate from and insert onto the bones of the pelvis. A majority of the stabilizers of the spine insert onto the pelvis, and the muscles that are responsible for hip movement originate from the pelvis.

And remember that there are muscles that act on the shoulder and knee that originate from the pelvis. If you recall, one of the five sites of origin for the latissimus dorsi is the posterior ilium, and it inserts onto the medial lip of the bicipital groove of the humerus. Thus, it assists in extension, adduction, and medial (internal) rotation of the shoulder. The sartorius and gracilis originate from bones of the pelvis and insert onto the medial, proximal tibia via the pes anserine tendon, assisting in movement of both the hip and knee. The rectus femoris and hamstrings also have origins with the pelvis and are primary movers for the tibiofemoral joint.

There are four pelvic joints: the lumbosacral joint, sacroiliac joint, pubic symphysis, and sacrococcygeal joint.

The Lumbosacral Joint

Functional Anatomy

The lumbosacral joint consists of the sacrum and the fifth lumbar vertebrae (L-5) (6.26).

 

6.26 The lumbosacral joint

 

This articulation is really a joint-by-association in regard to the pelvis. While the sacrum is not one of the three bones of the pelvis, it articulates with it and plays a vital role in posture and all daily functional movements.

Supporting Connective Tissue

Between the articulating surfaces of the sacrum and the L-5 vertebrae is an intervertebral disc that absorbs shock and dissipates the forces from the upper and lower body. The joint is supported by the lumbosacral ligament (6.27) and, to a lesser extent, the sacroiliac ligaments (6.28, 6.29).

The Sacroiliac Joint

Functional Anatomy

The sacroiliac (SI) joint is positioned between the sacrum and the ilium bones. There is an SI joint on each side of the sacrum. (6.30). It is the only articulation that connects the upper and lower body and serves to transfer weight from the upper body to the lower limbs.

 

6.30 The sacroiliac joint

 

When we are standing, walking, or running, this articulation transfers the weight of the body to the lower extremity. This transfer of weight results in several types of forces acting upon the SI joint: rotation, torsion, shearing, compression, and tension. Thus, it is under constant stress and strain.

Supporting Connective Tissue

The sacroiliac joint is a synovial joint and is supported by the anterior and posterior sacroiliac ligaments (6.31, 6.32).

 

6.31 The anterior sacroiliac ligament

6.32 The posterior sacroiliac ligament

Clinical Relevance of the Sacroiliac Joint

The SI joint gets a lot of attention but, interestingly, does not account for the majority of pain in the low back. It is estimated that only 15%–30% of all back pain is associated with this articulation.4 However, it does deserve the attention it receives because injuries to this joint are often detrimental to daily functional movements. For example, a sprain to the anterior and/or posterior sacroiliac ligaments is one injury that may cause low back pain in an individual. It can be caused by trauma, such as a football tackle, or by repetitive forces, in sports such as golf, figure skating, and gymnastics.

The Pubic Symphysis

Functional Anatomy

The term symphysis means to join together. In the pubic symphysis, the pubic bones come together and create an immovable joint (6.33). This provides great stability for the articulation to resist the compressive, tensile, and shearing forces acting on it during all of our daily activities.

6.33 The pubic symphysis

 

The forces acting on the pubic symphysis shift according to our body position. Try this: Begin in a seated position and note the compressive forces acting on the pubic symphysis. Next, move into a standing position. You should feel that same compression but also detect the tensile forces on the inferior aspect of the pubic symphysis. Finally, shift your weight and assume a single-leg stance. The compression continues to act on the pubic symphysis, but now you should experience a shearing force as well. Just imagine the shift in forces acting upon the pubic symphysis as the running back in our story ran down the field—a constant push and pull.

Supporting Connective Tissue

Four ligaments—superior pubic, inferior pubic, anterior pubic, and posterior pubic—and a fibrocartilaginous disc lend support to the pubic symphysis (6.34, 6.35). The medial edges of the pubic bones are protected by hyaline cartilage.

 

6.34 Superior pubic ligament, inferior pubic ligament, and anterior pubic ligament

6.35 Posterior pubic ligament

 

Clinical Relevance of the Pubic Symphysis

As clinicians, we do treat injuries that occur to the pubic symphysis. Symphyseal pain may be a result of the increased compressive forces that occur when we are seated or standing. As we shift from that balanced standing position to a one-legged stance, not only do the compressive forces increase but the shearing forces acting on the joint do as well. Injuries to the pubic symphysis may be caused by mechanisms such as overuse, sudden impact, and/or excessive stretching or twisting.

  • Overuse: In sports that involve a great deal of running or kicking, such as soccer, football, or martial arts, the repeated impact and strain can cause injury to the pubic symphysis.

  • Sudden impact: In contact sports such as football, rugby, or martial arts, a sudden, direct impact to the pelvic area can cause injury to the pubic symphysis.

  • Excessive stretching or twisting: Activities that involve a high degree of stretching or twisting of the lower body, such as gymnastics or dance, could potentially cause a strain or sprain of the pubic symphysis.

The Sacrcoccygeal Joint

Functional Anatomy

The apex of the sacrum and the base of the coccyx (“cuckoo beak”) join together to form the sacrococcygeal joint (6.36).

 

6.36 The sacrococcygeal joint

 

The sacrococcygeal joint is a symphysis joint with very limited mobility, as it only allows for passive flexion and extension.

Supporting Connective Tissue

Similar to the pubic symphysis, this articulation has a disc and hyaline cartilage to help protect it from compressive forces. The joint is additionally supported by five ligaments: intercornual, anterior sacrococcygeal, superficial posterior sacrococcygeal, deep posterior sacrococcygeal, and lateral sacrococcygeal.
