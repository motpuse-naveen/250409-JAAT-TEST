The Joint Capsule

Each synovial joint is contained within a capsule, or a sac-like structure. Imagine it is a hot summer day, and you are having a water balloon fight with your friends. That water balloon is similar to the makeup of the joint capsule. The outside of the balloon is the fibrous joint capsule—the structure forming the boundary and shape of the capsule. The inside surface of the balloon is the synovial membrane. Just like the balloon, the synovial membrane serves as the periphery for the joint cavity (space within the balloon) and contains the synovial fluid (the water in the balloon) (7.16).

 

7.16 The joint capsule of the knee (left). The structure of a water balloon (right) is similar to a joint capsule. Think of the outside surface as the capsule; the inside surface as the synovial membrane; the inside space as the joint cavity; and the water as the synovial fluid.

 

The synovial fluid within the joint cavity is essential for fluid movement and a healthy joint. It provides lubrication (think of engine oil) so the bones can move easily throughout knee flexion and extension without any friction (7.17).

 

7.17 Like oil in an engine, synovial fluid provides lubrication for the joint.

 

As the bones move from an open-packed position to a closed-packed position, the synovial fluid moves out of the way to allow the bones to become more congruent with one another. This allows for an easy, fluid knee motion. However, if you have been immobilized in a cast for a long period of time, the synovial fluid becomes viscous (thickened) and it does not easily move out of the way to allow for ease of movement (7.18). The femur is protected by hyaline (articular) cartilage, and synovial fluid provides nutrients to this connective tissue.

 
Functions of the synovial fluid
  • Lubricates the joint

  • Provides nutrients—in particular, to the hyaline cartilage

 

7.18 If your leg has been immobilized, the synovial fluid thickens and movement is not as easy.
