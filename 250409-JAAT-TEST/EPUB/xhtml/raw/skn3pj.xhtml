The Temporomandibular Joint

When you are chewing, talking, or displaying surprise at how much you are learning about anatomy, you are using your temporomandibular joint (TMJ) to facilitate those actions. The TMJ is a critically important articulation in the body, as we rely on it for nourishment, breathing, communication, expression, and balance (5.18). Balance? How is that possible? Read on and you will discover the reason.

 

5.18 How might the TMJ affect balance?

 

Functional Anatomy

The bony structure of the temporomandibular joint is simply the articulation between the mandible and the temporal bone (5.19). The condyle of the mandible (5.20) connects with the glenoid fossa of the temporal bone (see 5.22). You may be thinking, hold on a second—is the glenoid fossa (cavity) not part of the scapula? Yes, that is correct, but the aspect of the temporal bone that articulates with the condyle of the mandible is also called the glenoid fossa. (That can be confusing, but you got this!)

 

5.19 The temporomandibular joint (TMJ)

 

5.20 The mandible

 

The functions of the TMJ are many and complex. Think of how often it is in use; rarely is it at complete rest. Even when you are sleeping, the joint may be hard at work if you are someone who grinds their teeth at night. Another one of those complexities is that the TMJ also helps maintain the appropriate pressure of your middle ear. So it also has an effect on your equilibrium.

It is easy to think of the TMJ as a hinge-like joint because it appears to simply open and close. However, it does much more than that. It is a diarthrodial articulation—a freely moveable joint that affords bilateral or ipsilateral movement. As we open and close our mouths, we experience anterior and posterior sliding, rotation, translation, elevation, depression, or combinations of these movements (5.21).

 

5.21 A 3D view of the temporomandibular joint

 

Place your fingers along the TMJ and take a moment to open and close your mouth. Next, try to move your mandible into different positions. Can you feel it slide, rotate, and translate? Do you notice any clicking and/or popping when opening your mouth? Does your TMJ shift to one side or the other? If so, you may have temporomandibular joint disorder (more on that later).

Supporting Connective and Soft Tissue

The TMJ needs considerable support for stabilization and protection from the many forces acting upon it. An articular disc (5.22), several ligaments, and various muscles support and protect these articulations. Plural? Do we have more than one TMJ? Yes, we have a TMJ on either side of the head.

The articular disc is positioned between the mandibular condyle and the articular surface of the temporal bone. It plays a critical role in preventing TMJ dysfunction, as it decreases and displaces the forces placed upon it.

5.22 Articular disc of the temporomandibular joint

 

Ligamentous support for the TMJ is provided by four ligaments (5.23, 5.24):

  • Sphenomandibular ligament

  • Stylomandibular ligament

  • Pterygomandibular ligament

  • Lateral temporomandibular ligament

Muscular support for the TMJ comes from four muscles (5.25):

  • Temporalis

  • Masseter

  • Medial pterygoid

  • Lateral pterygoid

Clinical Relevance of the Temporomandibular Joint

Temporomandibular joint disorder (TMD) does not have one etiology; rather, a vast collection of conditions can cause it. Subluxation, arthritis, bruxism, and muscular disorders of the TMJ are just a few etiologies that may lead to TMD. But all include similar signs and symptoms such as pain in the TMJ, restrictive mandibular movement, TMJ sounds and sensations such as popping and cracking, headaches, and balance issues (5.26).

 

5.26 The TMJ. Temporomandibular joint disorder can have symptoms such as pain, restrictive mandibular movement, and popping and cracking, among others.

 

An easy and quick way to determine if you (or your patient) has clicking or popping that you may not hear is to palpate the TMJ. Try this out on yourself: Place your index and middle fingers on each of your TMJs. Now, open and close your mouth. Do you feel it pop? Click? Or even shift ever so slightly? Next, place the tips of your index fingers into your external auditory/acoustic meatus and open and close your mouth again. Do you feel any pops, cracks, or shifts? You may have TMD if you experience any of those sensations. If so, you are not alone. It is estimated that 31% of adults suffer from TMD.2 The good news is that it may resolve on its own or with conservative treatment such as rest, soft foods, passive stretching, and custom mouthpieces. Additionally, stress reduction can go a long way to relax the muscles supporting the TMJ. Find your inner zen (5.27)!
