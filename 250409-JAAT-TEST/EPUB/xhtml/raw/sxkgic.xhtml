Linking It All Together

Just as the human body is linked together by the kinetic chain, let us link what we learned in Chapter 5. A good way to do that is to revisit the questions from the beginning of the chapter to inspire discussion with your peers and instructor. We can also link to current evidence to further explore the questions.

To link to generalized discussion points online, go to booksofdiscovery.com, “For Students.”

  • Can you identify the muscles of mastication that support the temporomandibular joint (TMJ)? Why does the TMJ crack or pop sometimes?

    • While there many possible answers to this question, one answer can be:

      • You may suffer from temporomandibular disorder (TMD), a group of conditions affecting the muscles of mastication and the temporomandibular joint (TMJ).

    • To link to an example of current evidence to support a discussion, go to:

Kushagra Maini and Anterpreet Dua, “Temporomandibular Syndrome,” StatPearls, updated January 30, 2023, https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK551612/.

  • Damage to which nerves may cause vertigo, and how long can it last?

    • While there is not a simple answer to this question, here is a quick overview:

      • Someone with vertigo experiences a sensation of motion, usually a rotational motion. It indicates there is a vestibular dysfunction and, according to the following resource, vertigo is not permanent, as the central nervous system adapts over days to weeks.

    • To link to an example of current evidence to support a discussion, go to:

Monica Stanton and Andrew M. Freeman, “Vertigo,” StatPearls, updated March 18, 2022, https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK482356/.

  • Can you identify the cranial nerves and their respective senses that are most likely lost or diminished after trauma to the head?

    • It is your sense of smell as the olfactory nerve may be impacted with trauma to the head.

    • To link to an example of current evidence to support a discussion, go to:

Jessica Howell, Richard M. Costanzo, and Evan R. Reiter, “Head Trauma and Olfactory Function,” World Journal of Otorhinolaryngology - Head Neck Surgery 4, no. 1 (March 2018): 39–45, https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6051255/
