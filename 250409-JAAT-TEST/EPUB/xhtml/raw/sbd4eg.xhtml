The Ankle and Foot Complex

Try this: Stand up and shift your weight from one leg to the other. Do you notice how much you rely on your ankle for support and stability? The ankle and foot form a rather complex system of interrelated articulations. This complex contributes to the movement, balance, proprioception, weight distribution, shock absorption, protection, and leverage in the human body—the whole body, not just in your leg! An injury to the ankle or foot can negatively impact any aspect of the human kinetic chain.

Imagine a major league baseball pitcher with an ankle or foot injury attempting to throw a 90-mph fastball and be precise enough to hit the low and inside corner of the strike zone (7.57). He could not do it. The pitcher would not be able to generate enough force from the lower extremity to enable the shoulder to throw with much velocity. Also, the injured ankle or foot would prevent the pitcher from achieving the proper postural position to ensure precision and accuracy of his pitch. This tsunami of linked kinetic chain events places greater stress on the shoulder complex and increases the risk of injury to the throwing arm. Imagine—the ankle complex can influence any activity from the toes all the way through the fingers.

Functional Anatomy

Before we discuss the individual joints of the ankle and foot complex, we should review the forefoot, midfoot, and hindfoot/rearfoot regions (7.58).

 

7.58 Regions of the foot

 

The forefoot includes 14 phalanges, five metatarsals, and two sesamoid bones. The first digit is the main stabilizer when walking. The sesamoid bones play an integral role in balance, as they sit directly beneath the head of the first metatarsal (7.59, 7.60).

7.60 Plantar view of forefoot

 

The midfoot consists of five tarsal bones: the navicular, the cuboid, and the three cuneiforms. The cuneiforms are easy to locate as they align with the first three metatarsals, respectively (7.61, 7.62). The calcaneus and talus are considered the hindfoot or rearfoot. This is where the major joints (talocrural and subtalar) of the ankle complex are located.

How do we move forward, sideways, backwards, or diagonally during physical activity? That ultimately depends upon the ability of our ankle and foot to perform the actions of abduction, adduction, dorsiflexion, plantar flexion, inversion, eversion, supination, and pronation. To be able to complete almost any physical activity, including a non-weight-bearing activity such as swimming, these motions will help us achieve the ideal biomechanics necessary for movement. Now is a good time to clarify supination and pronation of the ankle and foot before we continue to move forward (or backwards, sideways, or diagonally) (7.63, 7.64).
