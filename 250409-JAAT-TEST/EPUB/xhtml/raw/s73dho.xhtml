Linking It All Together

Just as the human body is linked together by the kinetic chain, let us link what we learned in Chapter 7. A good way to do that is to revisit the questions from the beginning of the chapter to inspire discussion with your peers and instructor. We can also link to current evidence to further explore the questions.

To link to generalized discussion points online, go to booksofdiscovery.com, “For Students.”

  • Why do you suppose some athletes tear their ACLs and others do not?

    • While there many possible answers to this question, a few may include:

      • There are many dynamic forces acting upon the ACL when we jump or cut side to side; thus, athletes who participate in such activities may be more susceptible to injury.

      • Some athletes lack good landing mechanics from a jump and have difficulty sustaining the forces acting upon the ACL.

      • Some athletes may have poor technique, lack specific training and mobility exercises to prepare them for the stresses of the activity, and/or experience muscular fatigue.

      • Some individuals are born with narrow femoral notches. The femoral notch (intercondylar fossa) is a groove at the distal end of the femur. Stabilizing structures such as the cruciate ligaments and meniscofemoral ligaments are located here.

    • To link to an example of current evidence to support a discussion, go to:

Craig Pfeifer et al., “Risk Factors Associated with Non-Contact Anterior Cruciate Ligament Injury: A Systematic Review,” International Journal of Sports Physical Therapy 13, no. 4 (August 2018): 575–87, https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6088120/.

Joseph L. Yellin et al., “Narrow Notch Width Is a Risk Factor for Anterior Cruciate Ligament Injury in the Pediatric Population: A Multicenter Study,” Arthroscopy, Sports Medicine, and Rehabilitation 3, no. 3 (June 2021): e823–28, https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8220619/.

  • What supporting structures assist in keeping your patella in its tract?

    • To quickly identify a few:

      • Iliotibial band (ITB), vastus lateralis, vastus medialis oblique, and the patellofemoral ligament

    • To link to an example of current evidence to support a discussion, go to:

Charles A. Baumann, Betina B. Hinckel, and Miho J. Tanaka, “Update on Patellofemoral Anatomy and Biomechanics,” Operative Techniques in Sports Medicine 27, no. 4 (December 2019), https://doi.org/10.1016/j.otsm.2019.150683.

  • What do you think might be some commonly injured supporting structures of the knee, ankle, and foot in runners?

    • To quickly identify a few:

      • Iliotibial band (ITB), patella tendon, fibularis (peroneal) muscle group, gastrocnemius, tibialis posterior, soleus, plantar calcaneofibular ligament, and plantar fascia
