Supporting Connective Tissue for the Tibiofemoral Joint

Collateral Support

Collateral support for the tibiofemoral joint is provided by the lateral collateral ligament (LCL), the iliotibial tract or band (ITB), and the medial collateral ligament (MCL).

Functional Anatomy of the Lateral Collateral Ligament, Iliotibial Band, and Medial Collateral Ligament

The lateral aspect of the tibiofemoral joint is supported by the lateral collateral ligament (LCL) (7.19), also known as the fibular collateral ligament, and the iliotibial tract or band (ITB) (7.20).

 

7.19 The lateral collateral ligament (LCL)

 

Structure of the LCL

  • A round, cordlike ligament that lies under the biceps femoris tendon

  • Extends distally from the lateral femoral epicondyle to the head of the fibula, crossing over the lateral joint line

  • Sits just slightly more posteriorly along the joint line

Functions of the LCL

  • Provides lateral support of the tibiofemoral joint

  • Protects against a varus force acting upon the knee

  • Is taut when the knee is in extension and becomes more lax in flexion—especially after 30° of flexion and into full flexion

  • Is afforded more protection when the knee is in the flexed position, as it is protected by the biceps femoris bursa

7.20 The iliotibial band (ITB)

 

Structure of the ITB

  • A long, expansive connective tissue that is a continuation of the tensor fasciae latae along the lateral aspect of the thigh

  • Inserts at Gerdy’s tubercle (tibial tubercle) on the proximal, lateral tibia

Functions of the ITB

  • Provides lateral stability for the thigh and tibiofemoral joint, particularly as the knee moves into full extension. A great example of this is during the heel strike phase of the gait cycle (7.21).

 

7.21 During the heel strike phase of the gait cycle, the knee is in full extension.

 

The medial aspect of the tibiofemoral joint is supported by the medial collateral ligament (MCL), also known as the tibial collateral ligament (7.22, 7.23).

 

7.22 The medial collateral ligament (MCL)

 

7.23 A 3D view of the tibiofemoral joint

 

Structure of the MCL

  • Has a broad, triangular shape and consists of two parts: the superficial fibers and the deep fibers.

  • Both the superficial and deep fibers extend from the medial femoral epicondyle.

  • The superficial fibers cross over the medial joint line and insert at the proximal, medial tibia just under the pes anserine tendon.

  • The deep fibers insert into the periphery of the medial meniscus.

  • Sits more posterior than anterior along the medial joint line (just like the LCL).

Functions of the MCL

  • Provides medial support of the tibiofemoral joint.

  • Protects against valgus forces.

  • Fibers of the MCL are taut when the knee is in extension.

    • When the knee is in a flexed position, the anterior fibers are taut.

    • When the knee is in the mid-range position, the posterior fibers are taut.
       

Clinical Relevance of the Lateral Collateral Ligament, Iliotibial Band, and Medial Collateral Ligament

The lateral collateral ligament is rarely injured in isolation due its close proximity to the anterior cruciate ligament and the posterior cruciate ligament. Approximately 40% of LCL injuries occur when participating in contact sports.1 Injury involving the iliotibial band is most common in runners and accounts for 5%–14% of running-related injuries.2 The medial collateral ligament is the most commonly injured ligament involving trauma to the knee. 3 It accounts for approximately 7.9% of all knee injuries involving sports 4 and is more common in males than females at a 2:1 ratio.5 Let us look at some examples of LCL, ITB, and MCL injuries or conditions.

The LCL is often injured from a varus force that impacts the medial aspect of the knee complex and translates stress to the lateral aspect of the tibiofemoral joint (7.24).


7.24 Injury to the LCL from a varus force

 

ITB syndrome is a nontraumatic chronic condition commonly seen in runners (7.25). Pain is typically present along the lateral aspect of the knee at and/or just below the femoral epicondyle—particularly as the knee moves into full extension during the foot strike and early stance phases of the gait cycle.

Sagittal Plane and Oblique Plane Support

Providing support in the sagittal plane and the oblique plane, the anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) and posterior cruciate ligament (PCL) are the primary rotary stabilizers of the tibiofemoral joint (7.27). Both also assist in preventing hyperextension of the knee.

Functional Anatomy of the Anterior Cruciate Ligament

Let us start with the functional anatomy of the anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) (7.28).

 

7.28 The anterior cruciate ligament (ACL)

 

Structure of the ACL

  • Originates from the medial aspect of the lateral femoral condyle

  • Inserts into the middle of the intercondylar area of the tibia

Functions of the ACL

  • Prevents anterior displacement of the tibia away from the femur

  • Is a primary internal and external rotary stabilizer of the tibiofemoral joint

  • Prevents hyperextension of the knee
     

Clinical Relevance of the Anterior Cruciate Ligament

An injury to the anterior cruciate ligament seems prevalent in the sporting world, but it is actually a “fluke” injury. Consider this: an individual can tear their ACL when simply stepping off a curb, yet a soccer player can play their whole career and never experience an ACL injury. Now is a good time for a quick review of the anatomy for an explanation.

The ACL is least taut between 30° and 60° of knee flexion and most taut in full knee extension. Because of this, the ACL is often injured during the heel strike with the knee at or near the closed-packed position, particularly when there is a sudden deceleration just before a change in direction.

For example, suppose a basketball player dribbling quickly down the court (7.29) suddenly stops (decelerates), pivots (creating a valgus force), and is in the heel strike phase of the gait cycle (close to full knee extension). These forces acting upon the tibiofibular joint may cause damage to the ACL, medial and/or lateral meniscus, and medial collateral ligament and/or lateral collateral ligament. This is called a non-contact mechanism of injury (7.30, 7.31, 7.32).

 

7.29 Dribbling a basketball quickly down the court is an activity that may potentially lead to a non-contact ACL injury.

 

7.30 Anterior view of a non-contact mechanism of injury to the ACL

 

7.31 Medial view of a non-contact mechanism of injury to the ACL

 

7.32 Another example of a non-contact mechanism of injury is illustrated by a volleyball player who jumps up to hit the ball. As they come down, they land on a fully extended knee, potentially causing damage to the ACL.

 

Earlier we mentioned the Unhappy Triad when talking about meniscus injuries. This “old school” group—anterior cruciate ligament, medial collateral ligament, and medial meniscus—can be injured through a non-contact mechanism of injury. How is this possible? Both deceleration and rotational stress are acting on the ACL, and the valgus force is acting upon the MCL. If you recall, the deep fibers of the MCL attach to the medial meniscus. So excessive valgus force may cause those fibers to pull on the medial meniscus. Now we have damage to this member of the Unhappy Triad as well. As the saying goes, misery loves company!

Why is the Unhappy Triad an “old school” group? Simply put, the lateral compartment of the tibiofemoral joint was not often considered to be injured with this type of mechanism of injury. However, current research shows that the Unhappy Triad is actually a tetrad of injury, involving damage to the ACL, MCL, medial/lateral meniscus, and the anterolateral compartment of the tibiofemoral joint (7.33).6 So we can call the “new school” group the Unhappy Tetrad!

 

7.33 The Unhappy Tetrad

 

The Unhappy Tetrad

  • Anterior cruciate ligament

  • Medial collateral ligament

  • Medial/lateral meniscus

  • Anterolateral tibiofemoral compartment

The ACL can also be injured as a result of contact, such as a direct blow to the knee creating a valgus collapse of the knee or an anterior translation of the tibia. For example, a football player may take a direct hit to the lateral aspect of his knee, creating a valgus force to the medial aspect of the knee while the knee was near full extension. Or a rugby player in a scrum may have another player roll into her firmly planted lower leg, with the knee in full extension, causing the tibia to move forward away from the femur. These are contact mechanisms of injury (7.34).

 

7.34 A direct hit to the lateral aspect of the knee can be a contact mechanism of injury to the ACL.

 

Now that we have looked at various mechanisms of injury that may cause damage to the ACL, you can see that it truly takes a tsunami of events happening in the right place at the right time—or, we might say, the wrong place at the wrong time!

Functional Anatomy of the Posterior Cruciate Ligament

Moving on to the posterior cruciate ligament (PCL), note again that the PCL is also a primary rotary stabilizer of the tibiofemoral joint and assists in preventing hyperextension of the knee (7.35).

7.35 The posterior cruciate ligament (PCL)

 

Structure of the PCL

  • Originates from the anterolateral aspect of the medial femoral condyle

  • Inserts into the posterior tibia

  • Connects to the menisci via the ligament of Wrisberg

Functions of the PCL

  • Prevents posterior displacement of the tibia away from the femur

  • Is a primary internal and external rotary stabilizer of the tibiofemoral joint

  • Prevents hyperextension of the knee

Clinical Relevance of the Posterior Cruciate Ligament

The PCL is less often injured than the ACL, and injury to the PCL tends to be less debilitating (7.36). As noted, the PCL prevents posterior displacement of the tibia away from the femur.

 

7.36 Landing on an extended leg can lead to a PCL tear.

 

Knee hyperflexion is the most common injury to the PCL, followed by a trauma to the anterior tibia with the knee in a hyperflexed position. Imagine a downhill skier flying down the mountain: she loses control and her knee is forced into a hyperflexed position (7.37). This is an example of a non-contact mechanism of injury due to hyperflexion of the knee.

 

7.37 If this skier’s knee is forced into a hyperflexed position, this can be a non-contact mechanism of injury to the PCL.

 

Now imagine that you are a passenger in your friend’s car when you come to an abrupt stop—into the car in front of you. The momentum of the sudden stop forces your knee to crash into the dashboard, resulting in a PCL tear (a “dashboard injury”). The direct force to the tibia from colliding with the dashboard causes a hyperflexed knee, resulting in a torn PCL (and, perhaps, a fractured patella). This is an example of a contact mechanism of injury to the PCL (7.38). A direct blow to the anterior tibia—as can happen when a football player is being blocked—can also result in a PCL tear (7.39).

 

7.38 On the left, the posterior cruciate ligament (PCL) is intact. On the right, the PCL is completely torn after a collision with a dashboard has hyperflexed the knee—a contact mechanism of injury.

 

7.39 A blow to the anterior tibia can cause PCL damage.
