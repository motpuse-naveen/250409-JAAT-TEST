The Patellofemoral Joint

Functional Anatomy

The patellofemoral joint seems relatively simple in nature. It is where the posterior aspect of the patella articulates with the distal, anterior surface of the femur—specifically, the trochlear surface (7.40, 7.41). However, this articulation is quite complex and commonly injured.

It is critical for the patella to remain in its “track” to maintain a more congruent fit so the patella does not rub or scrape against the trochlear surface of the femur. Think of it as a train on its track (7.42)—and how important it is for the train to stay on the track for the safety of the train cars and well-being of its passengers!

 

7.42 Like a train, the patella needs to stay on its track to function properly.

 

Supporting Connective and Soft Tissue

The patella is the largest sesamoid bone in the human body, which means it is a free-floating bone. What keeps it in place? Because the patellofemoral joint has a shallow and incongruent fit, it relies on both connective and soft tissue for stability.

The quadriceps muscle group (7.43), pes anserine muscle group (7.44), and biceps femoris (7.45) assist in maintaining proper alignment of this articulation. If this alignment is altered in any way and the patella moves out of its normal tracking position, the bone can degenerate, resulting in a very painful condition of the knee.

The quadriceps provide a superior stabilizing force for the patellofemoral joint. The patellar ligament is the inferior stabilizing force (7.46). Laterally, the patellofemoral joint is stabilized by the iliotibial band (ITB) (7.47), lateral patellofemoral ligament, joint capsule, and the lateral retinaculum (7.46). Medially, the patellofemoral joint is stabilized by the medial patellofemoral ligament, medial retinaculum, and vastus medialis oblique (7.46, 7.48).

So, as you can see, the patella is held securely in place—and in its track—by numerous structures. An injury or deficiency to any one may have a significant impact upon the health of the patellofemoral joint.

Clinical Relevance of the Patellofemoral Joint

Why is it important to understand the positioning of the patella in regard to the femur? Let us revisit the track that the patella rides along when moving from flexion to extension and vice versa. If the ITB is taut, it will pull the patella laterally, causing it to deviate from its track—never a good thing for the human body (or a train).

On the medial aspect, deficiency in two soft tissues may also contribute to lateral tracking of the patella. If the vastus medialis oblique is weak or fires (contracts) late, the patella will again be pulled laterally out of its track. If the medial patellofemoral ligament is weakened or torn, its ability to limit lateral translation of the patella will be diminished and further increase the incongruence of the patellofemoral joint.

The balance between medial and lateral stability is critical in maintaining proper alignment of the patella and proper biomechanics of the patellofemoral joint. Any abnormal tracking of the patella has the potential to lead to increased and unnecessary stress on the joint (7.49). We need to keep that train rollin’ along on its track!
