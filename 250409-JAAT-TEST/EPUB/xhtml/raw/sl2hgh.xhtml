The Subtalar Joint

Functional Anatomy

As noted previously, only two tarsal bones compose the subtalar joint: the talus and calcaneus (7.84, 7.85).

 

7.84 Lateral view of subtalar joint

 

7.85 Medial view of subtalar joint

 

So the subtalar joint seems pretty straightforward, right? It is not as straightforward as it may appear. The subtalar joint allows a combination of movements that are critical to maintaining balance and enhancing agility: inversion, eversion, adduction, abduction.

A notable skeletal structure providing support for the subtalar joint is the sustentaculum tali, or the talar shelf (7.86). Located on the medial aspect of the calcaneus, it serves three purposes. The first is to hold up the portion of the talus that extends much more medially than the body of the calcaneus. Second, the sustentaculum tali is an insertion point for the posterior tibialis muscle and the tibiocalcaneal ligament. Third, it serves as the origin of the plantar calcaneonavicular ligament (spring ligament). This is important to know when it comes to certain injuries, as we will soon see.

 

7.86 Sustentaculum tali

These combined movements of inversion, adduction, eversion, and abduction at the subtalar joint are minimal but critically important when walking and running, particularly on uneven terrain.

Supporting Connective Tissue

The subtalar joint relies on the very same ligaments for its support as the talocrural joint (7.88, 7.89, 7.90). In fact, these are its only sources of support, as no muscle attaches to the talus.

Clinical Relevance of the Subtalar Joint

As noted earlier, the sustentaculum tali is an insertion point for the posterior tibialis muscle and the tibiocalcaneal ligament. Because of these insertion points, an avulsion fracture to the sustentaculum tali could occur with forced eversion or pronation. Good information to keep on hand as a clinician!
