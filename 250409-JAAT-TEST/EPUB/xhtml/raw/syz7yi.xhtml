The Talocrural Joint

Functional Anatomy

The talocrural joint (the true ankle joint) is a hinge joint with motion primarily occurring in the sagittal plane (7.65). These motions are plantar flexion and dorsiflexion.

 

7.65 Hinge motions of the talocrural joint

 

The ankle mortise is composed of the inferior articulating surface of the tibia, the lateral and medial malleoli, and the dome of the talus (7.66). This skeletal structure contributes to a stable and congruent articulation that is also well supported by soft tissue. This is important, as the talocrural joint is a key stabilizer during weight-bearing activities—in other words, anytime we are not sleeping, sitting, or swimming!

 

7.66 The talocrural joint, or true ankle joint

 

In addition to the stability provided by the talocrural joint, the mobility of this articulation is critically important for physical activity. For example, if a football lineman or a power lifter does not have optimal dorsiflexion at the talocrural joint, they will not be able to get into the full squat position (7.67), and this will have a detrimental impact on their performance. It is amazing how such a simple movement can have a big impact on activities ranging from squatting to throwing a baseball.

 

7.67 A football lineman in an almost full squat position demonstrates dorsiflexion of the talocrural joint.

 

However, the talocrural joint is not an independent mover. It coordinates with the subtalar and distal tibiofibular joints to produce multiplanar movement across the rearfoot. These motions include

  • Plantar flexion and dorsiflexion (sagittal plane)

  • Inversion and eversion (frontal plane)

  • Adduction and abduction (transverse plane)

The subtalar joint relies on many of the same supporting soft tissues as the talocrural joint, often resulting in a shared injury. Perhaps one could refer to the subtalar joint as the baby sibling—smaller than and protected by the bigger sibling yet an equally essential member of the family!

Because of their distinct structures and functions, we will discuss both the subtalar and distal tibiofibular joints in much more depth later in this chapter.

Supporting Connective Tissue

Beyond the very stable skeletal structure of the talocrural joint, this joint also depends on a posse of connective tissues beyond muscles for support and stability. Critically important to the stability of the talocrural joint are the ligaments and joint capsule. The lateral aspect is supported by the anterior talofibular ligament, the calcaneofibular ligament, and the posterior talofibular ligament (7.68).

 

7.68 Lateral talocrural ligaments

 

Medially, the talocrural joint is supported by the anterior tibiotalar, tibionavicular, tibiocalcaneal, and posterior tibiotalar ligaments. These are known collectively as the deltoid ligament (7.69).

 

7.69 Medial talocrural ligaments (deltoid ligament)

 

Another ligament worth noting is the bifurcate ligament. This ligament splits into two branches, the dorsal calcaneocuboid and the dorsal calcaneonavicular (7.70). While the primary role of the bifurcate ligament is to limit supination, it is also a static stabilizer of the talocrural joint during the hypersupination that is often associated with lateral ankle sprains.

 

7.70 Bifurcate ligament

 

Clinical Relevance of the Talocrural Joint

A lateral ankle sprain—the most common of all ankle sprains—is often referred to as an inversion sprain, with damage to the laterally located ligaments. However, the mechanism of injury is more often a combination of inversion, plantar flexion, and foot adduction—supination. So perhaps the most appropriate terminology for a lateral ankle sprain is a supination sprain (7.71, 7.72).

 

7.71 Supination (inversion) sprain

 

7.72 Ligament damage from supination (inversion) sprains

 

A medial ankle sprain is less common in the talocrural joint; it is also called an eversion sprain. Just as the inversion sprain can be called a supination sprain, the medial ankle/eversion sprain may be more appropriately called a pronation sprain (7.73, 7.74). For example, a soccer player might step into a hole on the pitch, forcing the ankle and foot into eversion, dorsiflexion, and abduction—pronation. A pronation sprain may occur independent of or in conjunction with a supination sprain.

 

7.73 Pronation (eversion) sprain

 

7.74 Ligament damage from pronation (eversion) sprains

 

Let us take this one step (pun intended!) further. Perhaps, when the soccer player stepped into that hole on the pitch, they initially suffered a supination sprain. Intuitively, they overcompensated to minimize the impact of the forces acting on the lateral anatomical structures. The overcompensation resulted in increased forces now acting on the medial anatomical structures causing a secondary pronation sprain. Often in these cases, the secondary injury (pronation sprain) is of less severity than the primary injury (supination sprain). However, the soccer player now has two types of injuries at the talocrural joint. As a soccer coach might say, that is quite unlucky.

Why are supination (inversion) sprains more common than pronation (eversion) sprains? That is an easy one: because the fibula extends farther into the ankle complex than the tibia does (7.75). When the ankle complex is forced into supination, there is no hard end-feel on the medial side. Your ankle is relying on the firm end-feel of muscles and tendons or the capsular end feel of ligaments to limit excessive motion.
