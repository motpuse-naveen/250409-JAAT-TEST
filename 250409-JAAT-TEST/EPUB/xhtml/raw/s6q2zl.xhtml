The Acetabulofemoral Joint

Imagine the football player as he begins his rehabilitation journey. Initially, he is using crutches because he cannot bear weight on his right hip and lower extremity. Because of his hard work and dedication to his rehabilitation plan, he begins to see improved stability and balance with daily activities. This is largely due to the increased strength, proprioception, and range of motion in his hip.

The hip joint’s anatomical name is the acetabulofemoral joint (or articulation). It is also referred to as the coxal joint. The job of the hip joint is to transmit the forces it endures all day long from the axial skeleton to the lower extremity and vice versa; it truly is the middleman.

Consider all of the forces that act upon your hip throughout the day. It is always under stress—when you are standing, walking, swimming, squatting, sitting, and even sleeping. How does sleeping stress your hip? During sleep, you toss and turn. A change in your position impacts the amount of stress on your hip. Think of yourself in a side lying position—or try it now. Can you imagine (or feel) that increased stress upon your hip? Now suppose your hip is injured and then you roll over onto it. This would be very painful.

Let us look at the acetabulofemoral joint in more detail.

Functional Anatomy

Most often, the femur and acetabulum are identified as the bones of the acetabulofemoral joint. However, the structure is a bit more complex than that, as the acetabulum is the intersection of three bones: the ilium, ischium, and pubis (6.2). The acetabulum (concave structure) is a deep socket (think of a bowl) (6.3). It houses the femoral head (convex structure), affording a great deal of congruency between the innominate bones of the acetabulum and the femoral head. This congruency provides a significant amount of stability in the acetabulofemoral joint, while the ball-and-socket configuration of this articulation allows tremendous mobility. Both stability and mobility are key, given the demands placed upon the joint.

The acetabulofemoral joint is a multi-axial joint that allows for movement through the following planes of motion: sagittal, transverse, frontal, and oblique (6.4). In most of our daily activities, we move across two or more planes at the same time (6.5).

A healthy hip not only affords us phenomenal mobility, but it also provides us with critically important balance and stability. When walking or standing, the acetabulofemoral joint balances the upper body. As we move throughout our day, the articulation is also supporting and transferring the forces acting upon it from the ground—and for some, from opposing football players! It is a critical link in the kinetic chain and any injury to the hip will be noticeable, as one’s gait and stance will be impacted.
