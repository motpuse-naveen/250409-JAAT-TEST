The Wrist Complex

As we move on to the wrist complex, our focus will turn to two areas: the radiocarpal joint and the scaphoid (and related bones).

Before we dive into these two structures, it is also important to mention the ulnocarpal complex that is located within the wrist complex. It is more commonly referred to as the triangular fibrocartilage complex (TFCC) (3.18, 3.19). This elaborate system of connective tissue provides structure, allows for motion and stability of the wrist, and assists in disseminating the forces acting upon the wrist. The ulnocarpal disc (triangular disc) plays a prominent role in the stabilization of the TFCC.

The Radiocarpal Joint

Functional Anatomy

The radiocarpal joint is often referred to as the true wrist joint. It comprises the distal end of the radius, the scaphoid, the lunate, and, indirectly, the triquetrum (3.20). The triquetrum is connected via the triangular fibrocartilage (TFC), a cartilaginous disc. This disc also stabilizes the triquetrum in relation to both the scaphoid and lunate via connective tissues. Interestingly, no muscles insert into the scaphoid, lunate, or triquetrum, so they rely on surrounding forces for motion.

 

3.20 The radiocarpal joint

 

While the elbow allows us to position our hand to perform many daily activities, it is our wrist that truly perfects our ability to perform them. The radiocarpal joint is a rather stable joint that allows for multiplanar movement. Because it is an ellipsoid joint, the wrist is able to move into flexion, extension, abduction (radial deviation), adduction (ulnar deviation), or a combination of these movements.

Supination and pronation also occur at the wrist, but only in conjunction with the radius and ulna, with the radius doing the bulk of the work (approximately 90%) as it moves over the ulna (3.21).

 

3.21 Forearm supination and pronation

 

Take a moment to practice this: Place your right hand and wrist in the supinated position. Gently position your left hand over the ulna and radius of your right forearm. Move your right hand and wrist into a pronated position. Did you feel it? You really only feel the radius moving over the ulna; the ulna stays still. (Pretty cool, right?)

 

 

What are some daily activities that would be impacted if your radius was injured and you could not supinate or pronate?

  • Turning a doorknob
  • Tightening a screw
  • Unlocking your front door with a key

 

Supporting Connective Tissue

In addition to the supporting triangular fibrocartilage disc, a plethora of ligaments connect the radius to the scaphoid and lunate (3.22, 3.23).

  • The dorsal radiocarpal ligament supports the scaphoid during movement.

  • The short radiolunate ligament assists in limiting the motion of the lunate.
  • The long radiolunate ligament limits the motion of the lunate.
  • The radioscaphocapitate ligament assists in stabilizing the scaphoid.
  • The radioscapholunate ligament (ligament of Testut) is interesting. It is not a ligament, as it does not provide support for the scaphoid or lunate. What does it do? It acts like a gutter to allow blood to flow to the lunate. If the gutter gets clogged, avascular necrosis may occur in the lunate (also known as Kienböck’s disease).

Clinical Relevance of the Radiocarpal Joint

A common injury to this articulation is the Colles fracture. Typically a result of a FOOSH (fall-on-an-outstretched-hand) injury, this fracture occurs in the dorsal, distal radius (3.24). Astonishingly, the Colles fracture makes up 17.5% of all fractures seen in adults treated in an emergency room.4

3.24 Colles fracture

 

Another injury to the radiocarpal joint is the reverse Colles fracture, or Smith fracture. This fracture is also a result of falling on one’s wrist; however, the wrist is in a flexed position at the points of contact with ground, causing one to land on the dorsal surface of the hand. The fracture occurs at the distal radius but with displacement toward the palmar aspect of the hand (3.25).

The Scaphoid

The scaphoid arguably plays the most critical role in proper wrist function. Not only is it the largest of the carpal bones, but it is literally at the center of optimal wrist mechanics. It sits laterally to the lunate, which aligns with the third digit. The lunate is the most commonly dislocated bone.

Functional Anatomy

The scaphoid is a boat-shaped bone that has a proximal pole and a distal pole. The waist of the scaphoid separates the two poles (3.26). The waist of the scaphoid also serves as the floor of the anatomical snuffbox (3.27).

Clinical Relevance of the Scaphoid

While there are many injuries to the bones of the hand, the scaphoid is the most commonly fractured bone in the hand, so it makes sense to use it as our clinical relevance reference. While scaphoids account for only 2%–7% of all fractures, they account for 70% of carpal fractures. Of these, 65%–80% occur at the waist of the scaphoid, 20%–25% occur at the proximal pole, and 10% occur at the distal pole.7

A scaphoid fracture often results from a FOOSH (fall-on-an-outstretched-hand) injury (3.28). Imagine a cross country runner who trips and falls along a rugged trail. She lands on an outstretched hand. When she seeks out the athletic trainer for assistance, she will likely present with pain and swelling over the anatomical snuffbox, particularly over the waist of the scaphoid.

 

3.28 A FOOSH (fall-on-an-outstretched-hand) injury

 

Like the lunate, the scaphoid is susceptible to vascular issues. About 30%-40% of scaphoid fractures result in avascular necrosis (3.29).8 The scaphoid receives its blood supply from branches of the radial artery, which primarily enter through the distal aspect of the scaphoid. Very few branches enter through the proximal aspect. Therefore, there is a much higher incidence of avascular necrosis in the proximal pole and, to a lesser extent, the waist of the scaphoid. The distal pole has an even less likelihood of avascular necrosis.
