The Elbow Complex

When most people think of the elbow, they are probably thinking of the humeroulnar joint. Of the three joints in the elbow complex—humeroulnar, humeroradial, and proximal radioulnar—most injuries in the elbow complex occur within the humeroulnar joint. So this section will focus on this joint along with its supporting ligaments and bursa.

The Humeroulnar Joint

Functional Anatomy

Also known as the true elbow joint or trochlear joint, the humeroulnar joint (3.5) partly resembles an ice cream scoop (3.6). Simply put, the concave shape (the ice cream scooper) of the trochlear notch (3.7) rotates around the trochlea of the distal medial humerus. Because of this relatively congruent articulation, the hinge joint is fairly stable. The olecranon process of the ulna (3.5, 3.7) articulates fully with the trochlea, particularly in full extension of the elbow.

The humeroulnar joint allows us to position our hand for daily functions. Because of the rotating motion of the trochlear notch, this hinge joint allows for flexion and extension of the elbow. These motions allow us to carry out important functions such as eating, drinking, and bringing our hands together during the wind-up phase of overhand throwing (3.8).

 

3.8 Movement at the elbows allows this pitcher to bring his hands together in the wind-up phase of overhand throwing.

 

 

What kinds of activities cause valgus, varus, or valgus extension forces that impact the elbow complex?

Some examples:

  • A valgus force occurs while throwing a javelin. particularly, when the shoulder drops below 90°–100° of abduction placing more stress on the medial elbow.
  • A varus force may occur with a dislocation of the elbow resulting in damage to the radial collateral ligament, such as a gymnast landing on an outstretched hand with a locked elbow.
  • A valgus extension overload force may occur in a gymnast (3.9), tennis player, golfer, or baseball pitcher.

 

3.9 Repetitive stress on the elbow is a potential valgus extension overload force.

Supporting Connective Tissue

Two ligaments provide increased stability for the humeroulnar joint: the ulnar collateral ligament (UCL) and the radial collateral ligament (RCL). These are also referred to as the medial collateral ligament (MCL) and the lateral collateral ligament (LCL), respectively. However, the terms UCL and RCL are used more often, to avoid confusion with the MCL and LCL of the tibiofemoral joint (see Chapter 7).

Interestingly, both ligaments are composed of smaller ligaments. Let us begin with the ulnar collateral ligament (3.10), as this is more commonly injured than the radial collateral ligament.

 

3.10 Components of the ulnar collateral ligament

 

The three parts of the UCL are the anterior oblique ligament, the posterior oblique ligament, and the transverse ligament (also known as Cooper’s ligament). These ligaments are often referred to as “bundles”: the anterior bundle, the posterior bundle, and the transverse bundle.

  • The anterior bundle originates from the medial epicondyle of the humerus and inserts onto the coronoid process of the ulna.
    • An extension of the anterior bundle is the oblique cord, which inserts onto the proximal radius.
  • The posterior bundle also originates from the medial epicondyle but inserts onto the olecranon.
  • The transverse bundle (Cooper’s ligament) runs between the anterior and posterior bundles, originating from the coronoid process and inserting into the olecranon.

The anterior bundle is the primary medial stabilizer and, thus, the strongest stabilizer against the valgus and extension forces acting upon the elbow complex. Collectively, all three bundles play a role. Think of it like this: the anterior bundle is the lifeguard watching over us as we dive into the water, and the posterior and transverse bundles are the rescue buoys to help the lifeguard, if needed (3.11).

 

3.11 Like lifeguards and rescue buoys for swimmers, the bundles are ready to protect the elbow.

 

The UCL is also the floor to the cubital tunnel of the elbow, where the ulnar nerve runs through the center (3.12). The ulnar nerve can easily be compromised in this tunnel, particularly with valgus extension overload. The arcuate ligament, or Osborne’s ligament, serves as the roof of the tunnel. The tunnel has three borders: the anterior is the medial epicondyle, the posterior is the medial head of the triceps brachii, and the lateral is the olecranon. This creates a very narrow tunnel, making the ulnar nerve susceptible to impingement, should any of the musculoskeletal structures be injured.

3.12 Cubital tunnel and ulnar nerve

 

The radial collateral ligament complex provides the primary stability for the lateral elbow (3.13). It is referred to as a complex because it is just that—complex. The RCL complex is y-shaped as it fans out across the lateral elbow connecting the humerus, radius, and ulna.

 

3.13 Components of the radial collateral ligament complex

 

The four components of the RCL complex are the RCL itself and three accompanying ligaments: the annular ligament, the lateral collateral ulnar ligament, and the lateral collateral radial ligament.

  • The radial collateral ligament originates from the lateral epicondyle and inserts onto the annular ligament.
  • The annular ligament originates from the proximal ulna and inserts onto the head of the radius.
  • The lateral collateral ulnar ligament originates from the lateral epicondyle of the humerus and inserts onto the crest of the ulna.
  • The lateral collateral radial ligament also originates from the lateral epicondyle but inserts onto the head of the radius.

On the lateral aspect of the elbow complex, all components of the RCL protect against varus and posterolateral forces acting upon the elbow complex. Because the annular ligament wraps around the head of the radius, it also provides support for the proximal radioulnar joint. Also providing support for the proximal radioulnar joint is the oblique cord.

Clinical Relevance of the Humeroulnar Joint

Perhaps one of the most familiar injuries to the elbow complex involving the humeroulnar joint is an ulnar collateral ligament injury requiring the surgical procedure referred to as Tommy John surgery. It is a fairly common surgery among major league baseball pitchers. Why pitchers? Well, they experience tremendous repetitive stress along the medial aspect of the elbow complex while pitching. The UCL, specifically the anterior bundle, serves as the primary restraint against the valgus stresses incurred with overhand activities.

While this injury is most common among baseball pitchers it is not isolated to pitchers or the sports of baseball and softball. An injury to the UCL may occur in javelin throwers, quarterbacks, gymnasts, wrestlers, and even offensive linemen.

Tommy John surgery reconstructs the UCL using a graft from the palmaris longus tendon (3.14).

The Olecranon Bursa

Functional Anatomy

The olecranon bursa is located between the triceps brachii tendon and the olecranon (3.15). It acts as a buffer to protect the triceps brachii tendon from unnecessary stress, allowing the elbow to move freely between flexion and full extension (3.16).

Clinical Relevance of the Olecranon Bursa

The olecranon bursa is superficial and prone to injury from trauma, a secondary inflammatory condition, or an infection (3.17).

3.17 Swollen olecranon bursa (olecranon bursitis)

 

A great example of injury to the olecranon bursa is that of a baseball center fielder who dove to make a seemingly impossible catch. As they dove, they pulled their elbow into flexion, to be able to position their glove and make the catch. However, when they hit the ground, they landed directly on their left elbow. It was the inning-ending catch, and as the center fielder headed to the dugout, a teammate looked over and shouted, “What happened to your elbow?” The center fielder looked down and saw swelling the size and shape of a golf ball—an injured olecranon bursa.
