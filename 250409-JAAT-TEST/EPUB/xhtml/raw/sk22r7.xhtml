Head and Face

To accompany Trail Guide to the Body, Chapter 5, Head, Neck & Face

On a beautiful spring day, a field hockey player walked into the athletic training facility. She had awakened that morning with paralysis on the right side of her face. Yes, that is right—half of her face was frozen! She was unable to make many facial expressions. Fortunately, the athletic trainer understood that the player was experiencing transient facial nerve paralysis (Bell’s palsy).

After a few days of appropriate treatment including stimulation of the facial muscles, the athlete’s bright smile returned to normal. What caused the temporary paralysis, you may wonder? She simply slept on her face “wrong” and impinged the facial nerve.

As an athletic training and kinesiology instructor, I am often asked by students, “Why do we have to learn facial muscles and nerves? When will we ever use them in our clinical practice?” The field hockey player’s story illustrates that clinicians of all kinds need to know about structures in the head and face.

Based on your own observations and knowledge, think about the questions below. What can you guess at or hypothesize? You can test your answers as you read and when we revisit the questions at the end of the chapter.

  • Can you identify the muscles of mastication that support the temporomandibular joint (TMJ)? Why does the TMJ crack or pop sometimes?

  • Damage to which nerves may cause vertigo and how long can it last?

  • Can you identify the cranial nerves and their respective senses that are most likely lost or diminished after trauma to the head?

To help you learn more about key structures in the head and face, this chapter focuses on two main topics: the cranial nerves and the only moving articulation in the skull, the temporomandibular joint (TMJ).

From the experience of the field hockey player, you know that nerves move muscles. They also coordinate your sensory perception. Let us pause and review these nerves. Motor (efferent) nerves control your muscles and, therefore, movement. They carry signals from your brain to the muscle and, voilà, that body part moves! Sensory (afferent) nerves carry signals to your brain and allow you to experience sensations such as smell, taste, sight, and touch. When you touch a hot stove, sensory nerves immediately send a signal to your brain informing it that your skin is hot and in danger (5.1). An easy trick for remembering which nerves carry signals in which direction is this:

Efferent nerves: Exit the brain (the signals exit the brain)

Afferent nerves: Arrive at the brain (the signals enter the brain)

 

5.1 Sensory nerves protect the hand.

 

Continuing with Bell’s palsy (5.2), the field hockey player’s case was caused by an impinged facial nerve, which is both a motor nerve and a sensory nerve. As a clinician, you may also encounter patients who have Bell’s palsy as a result of a stroke. While it will likely take time, clinicians work with these individuals to return the facial muscles back to full or close-to-full function.

5.2 Bell’s palsy symptoms

 

For example, an athletic trainer working with such an individual suffering from Bell’s palsy may utilize a probe from an electrical muscle stimulation unit. Each facial muscle is stimulated with the probe as the patient attempts to create the facial expression associated with that muscle. If the goal is to retrain the risorius muscle to draw the mouth laterally, the athletic trainer stimulates the risorius with the probe and asks the patient to attempt a fake smile. But no one wants a fake smile—we want to communicate joy through a true or real smile (5.3). So, the athletic trainer also stimulates the zygomaticus major.

 

5.3 A true smile communicates joy–and that the nerves and muscles are working.
