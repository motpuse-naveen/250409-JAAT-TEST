Joints of the Elbow & Wrist Complexes

To accompany Trail Guide to the Body, Chapter 3, Forearm & Hand

The starting pitcher for a nationally competitive baseball team relished his role. Not only was he the ace pitcher, but he was also the team captain. He was a positive and strong leader, never wanting to let his team down and always pumping them up when challenges arose. He was first to appear at 5 a.m. practices, in the weight room, and on game days—and the last to leave.

How does this relate to the anatomy of either the elbow or wrist complex? Unbeknownst to anyone, including the athletic trainer, the pitcher was having pain in the elbow of his throwing arm. He figured he could just “work through the pain” and put some ice on it, and, eventually, the pain would go away. After all, he had a team to lead and a regional championship to win, to get his team to the World Series. As you can imagine, this plan was an epic failure. The pain in his elbow did not subside. Rather, the pain became substantially worse, and his throwing biomechanics were dramatically impacted.

Being well-versed in overhand throwing mechanics, the athletic trainer noticed that the pitcher’s throwing motion had ever-so-slightly changed during the late cocking phase of throwing. Unfortunately, the damage to the elbow had already been done. The pitcher was diagnosed with an ulnar collateral ligament (UCL) tear due to valgus extension overload. He underwent surgery for the torn UCL and remained dedicated to the individualized rehabilitation plan created for him by the athletic trainer. Because of this smarter approach, the pitcher returned the following season—and led his team to the College World Series.

In this chapter, we take a deep dive into the anatomy of the elbow, forearm, and wrist. Now that you have dipped your toes in the water with the pitcher’s true story, we will look more closely at the functional anatomy related to structures commonly injured in baseball or any overhead activity.

The elbow, forearm, and wrist play a vital role in our daily functional lives. They help us to do, to feel, and to communicate. They allow us to feed ourselves, dress ourselves, brush our teeth, comb our hair, and so much more. Not only does the hand let us know if the stove is too hot to touch or the water is too cold to dive into, but it also works with the wrist to help us communicate with others, in both positive ways (waving hello) and negative ways (indicating road rage) (3.1, 3.2).

The elbow complex has three joints: humeroulnar, humeroradial, and proximal (superior) radioulnar. The wrist complex includes the radiocarpal joint and the distal (inferior) radioulnar joint (3.3). This chapter focuses on the humeroulnar (true elbow) joint and the radiocarpal (true wrist) joint, along with some key surrounding structures.

 

3.3 Joints of the elbow and wrist complexes

 

Injuries to these anatomical regions are common. In sports-related activities such as baseball, elbow injuries occur frequently, second only to shoulder injuries.1 For example:

  • Lateral epicondylitis, also known as tennis elbow, is the most common cause of elbow pain, and it affects male and female individuals equally. It is typically caused by activities that require repetitive wrist extension, forearm supination, and/or radial deviation. Although its name suggests that it only affects tennis players, they make up about 10% of the population diagnosed with tennis elbow.2 So it will “serve” you well to understand the elbow anatomy that is associated with this overuse injury.
  • Most wrist injuries are caused when someone reaches out their hand to brace themselves from tripping or falling. This is referred to as a FOOSH injury—fall on an outstretched hand.
  • Men and women ice hockey players have the highest rate of hand and wrist injuries in intercollegiate sports, with most of the injuries occurring during competition rather than practice. Approximately 25% of all sports-related injuries involve the hand.3

Since the elbow, forearm, and wrist contribute significantly to our everyday functioning, any injury in these regions can have detrimental impact upon our well-being. As a clinician, you will need an in-depth understanding of the anatomy of the elbow and wrist complexes to better assist patients in resuming functional activities.
