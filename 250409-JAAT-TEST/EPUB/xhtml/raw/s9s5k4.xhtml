Linking It All Together

Just as the human body is linked together by the kinetic chain, let us link what we learned in Chapter 3. A good way to do that is to revisit the questions from the beginning of the chapter to inspire discussion with your peers and instructor. We can also link to current evidence to further explore the questions.

To link to generalized discussion points online, go to booksofdiscovery.com, “For Students.”

  • Elbow dislocations are common in physical activities. Which type of dislocation do you think might be most common in regard to the elbow? Why?
    • A posterior or posterolateral dislocation is the most common; 90% of all elbow dislocations are posterior or posterolateral.
    • Most elbow dislocations are a result of a FOOSH (fall-on-an-outstretched-hand) injury causing an axial load on the humeroulnar joint.
    • To link to an example of current evidence to support a discussion, go to:

James Layson and Ben J. Best, “Elbow Dislocation,” StatPearls, updated August 8, 2022, https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK549817/.

  • Why do so many overhand-activity athletes experience elbow injuries?
    • While many etiologies may contribute to elbow injuries, it is often a combination of factors and not a singular contributing factor. Of the many possible answers to this question, some include:
      • Individuals may experience fatigue in the shoulder, altering their biomechanics of throwing and placing increased valgus stress on the elbow complex.
      • Single-sport specialization has also been associated with valgus extension load injuries of the elbow.
    • To link to an example of current evidence to support a discussion, go to:

Austin M. Looney, Paolo D. Rigor, and Blake M. Bodendorfer, “Evaluation and Management of Elbow Injuries in the Adolescent Overhead Athlete,” SAGE Open Medicine 9 (April 22, 2021), https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33996078/.

  • Other than the ulnar collateral ligament, what structures in the elbow complex may be injured during the act of throwing?
    • There are several structures that could be impacted, including:
      • Ulnar nerve
      • Common flexor tendon
      • Medial epicondyle
    • To link to an example of current evidence to support a discussion, go to:

L. Pearce McCarty III, “Approach to Medial Elbow Pain in the Throwing Athlete,” Current Reviews in Musculoskeletal Medicine 12, no. 1 (March 2019), https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6388576/.
