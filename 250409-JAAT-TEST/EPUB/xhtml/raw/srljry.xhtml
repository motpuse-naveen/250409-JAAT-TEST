Supporting Connective Tissue

At any given time when we move our hip, the acetabulum covers 40% of the femoral head.1 It is incredible that a joint so mobile also has that much contact between its articulating bones. And recall that it provides great stability and mobility. But this skeletal feature needs help, as so many forces are acting upon the acetabulofemoral joint. In other words, the acetabulum needs to recruit a friend (or two or three!).

Acetabular Labrum

One of the first friends that will come to the aid of the acetabulum is the acetabular labrum. This labrum is a ring of fibrocartilage that runs along the rim of the acetabulum (6.6). It provides another level of support and stability. How does it do this? The ring of cartilage limits the head of the femur from rolling past the 40% coverage of the acetabulum and into extreme range of motion. The labrum serves to deepen the acetabulum and prevents excessive range of motion that may cause harm to the acetabulofemoral joint.

6.6 The acetabulofemoral joint, with the acetabular labrum highlighted in blue

Ligaments

The next requests for help will go out to the ligaments that support the acetabulofemoral joint. The capsule is supported by the three ligaments that are arranged in a spiral configuration to prevent hyperextension of the hip. The three ligaments are as follows:

  • The iliofemoral ligament (or Y ligament of Bigelow) is the strongest ligament in the human body. It extends from the anterior inferior iliac spine (AIIS) to the intertrochanteric line, inserting both superiorly and inferiorly along that line (6.7, 6.8). Because of the split fiber alignment, it also limits external rotation.

  • The pubofemoral ligament is located more medially and is deeper than the iliofemoral ligament (6.7). It originates from the superior pubic crest and inserts at the lower intertrochanteric line. It is at this spot where it joins the iliofemoral ligament. The pubofemoral ligament is smaller and more fragile than the iliofemoral ligament, so it is more prone to injury. Additionally, it helps to limit hip abduction.

  • The ischiofemoral ligament is a strong ligament that has a more posterior origin on the ischium (6.8). It also inserts onto the intertrochanteric line to join the iliofemoral and pubofemoral ligaments. This ligament limits hip adduction with flexion and internal rotation.

 

6.7 The iliofemoral and pubofemoral ligaments

6.8 The iliofemoral and ischiofemoral ligaments

 


 

 

So you can see how these ligaments all work together to prevent hyperextension of the hip—location, location, location (6.9)! When the hip is in the flexed position, all three ligaments are relaxed; however, when the hip is in the extended position, all three ligaments are taut.

Supporting Soft Tissue

Due to the complexity of the movements within the acetabulofemoral joint, there are many, many muscles that create the various movements of the hip. The muscles also play a vital role in helping to provide further stability of the hip. Let us take a look at a few: gluteus minimus, tensor fasciae latae (TFL) gluteus medius, gluteus maximus, iliopsoas, sartorius, the “deep six,” and the adductor group.

Gluteus Minimus

The smallest of the gluteal muscles, the gluteus minimus is located deep to the gluteus medius and shares a similar role in regard to stabilization of the hip (6.10). The primary action of the gluteus minimus is abduction of the hip, but it also contributes to medial (internal) rotation of the hip. It is most obviously at work when we shift our weight and assume a single-leg stance. In this weight-bearing position, the gluteus minimus works in conjunction with the tensor fasciae latae and gluteus medius muscles.

 

6.10 Gluteus minimus

Tensor Fasciae Latae

The tensor fasciae latae (TFL) (6.11) is a truly unique muscle as it contributes to the stabilization and mobility of both the hip and knee complexes. This may seem surprising because the muscle itself does not cross through the knee complex.

To better understand, let us review its origin and insertion. The TFL originates from the anterior superior iliac spine (ASIS) and the anterior iliac crest and inserts into the iliotibial tract or band (ITB) at about the level of the greater trochanter. At this level, the TFL also connects to the gluteus maximus via aponeurosis. The ITB inserts at Gerdy’s tubercle (tibial tubercle), located on the proximal, lateral aspect of the tibia. It is because of its connection to the ITB that the TFL contributes to the stabilization and motion of the tibiofemoral complex.

The TFL plays a huge role while we are walking, as it permits the non-weight-bearing hip to rise and allows the leg to clear the ground. The TFL works collaboratively with various muscles and connective tissue:

  • With the gluteus minimus and medius, the TFL creates medial (internal) rotation.

  • With the gluteus maximus, medius, and minimus, the TFL creates hip abduction.

  • With the rectus femoris, the TFL creates knee flexion once beyond 30° of flexion.

  • With the ITB, the TFL provides lateral stability of the tibiofemoral joint as the knee moves into full extension. Additionally, they also create lateral (external) rotation of the tibia. Both of these actions occur when a soccer player kicks the ball, for example.

 

6.11 Tensor fasciae latae

Gluteus Medius

In order to maintain a neutral pelvis and an upright trunk position when we are in a single-leg stance, walking, running, or throwing a baseball, we rely heavily on the gluteus medius (6.12). It is the primary hip stabilizer in these activities and is the primary hip abductor. It has a big job for a relatively small muscle when compared to the gluteus maximus. During the stance phase of walking, for example, the gluteus medius of the stance leg ensures the pelvis does not drop due to the unsupported leg in the swing phase. We may actually see the pelvis raised ever so slightly on the unsupported side—now that is a powerful muscle! And it is critically important for our daily functional activities.

 

6.12 Gluteus medius

Gluteus Maximus

The gluteus maximus is the largest and heaviest muscle in the human body (6.13). It is an incredibly powerful muscle and when it produces force, the hip benefits greatly. The gluteus maximus is a primary hip stabilizer, particularly when we rise from a seated position, straighten our trunks from a bent-over position, walk up a hill or a set of stairs, and run. It is the dominant hip extensor, but it works in conjunction with the gluteus medius to stabilize the pelvis when a person is standing on one leg, and it helps to stabilize the lower extremity due to its attachment to the tensor fasciae latae.

 

6.13 Gluteus maximus

Iliopsoas

The iliopsoas is often referred to as the primary hip flexor (6.14). To be fair to this trio of muscles, that is a bit of an oversimplification. While it is true the psoas major combines with the psoas minor and iliacus to form the iliopsoas muscle, and they work as a collective unit, each muscle plays a unique role in hip stability.

 

6.14 Iliopsoas

 

When the trio works together, they are critical for achieving proper standing or sitting lumbar posture and contribute to hip stability during walking and running. Acting independent of one another, the iliacus is more involved while we are running. It stabilizes the pelvis and is the driver of hip flexion. When we are in a seated position, the psoas major stabilizes the lumbar spine and takes the lead in generating initial hip flexion when we are standing or in the supine position.

What about the psoas minor? Here is a not-so-secret secret: it contributes to trunk flexion. While it does assist in hip flexion, this happens only through its association with the psoas major and iliacus. It connects to the fascia of the iliacus and to the distal tendon of the psoas major, and so it assists the other members of this trio, as they are the drivers of hip flexion. One last interesting bit of information is that only 40%–60% of the population has a psoas minor2—so it seems that not everyone needs one!

Sartorius

The sartorius is a long, strap-like muscle that runs along the most superficial anterior aspect of the thigh (6.15). In fact, it is the longest muscle in the human body and has an S-shaped curvature, affording it the ability to cross two complexes—the hip and the knee. Because of this, it contributes to five movements of the lower extremity. Those motions include hip flexion, lateral (external) rotation, and abduction as well as knee flexion and medial (internal) rotation.

 

6.15 Sartorius

 

To help you remember the actions of the sartorius, try this: Stand on your right leg, placing the plantar aspect of your left foot along the medial aspect of the right knee complex (6.16). In assuming this position, you have achieved all five movements of the sartorius!

The piriformis is unique in that it sits atop the sciatic nerve (more about that to come). Let us first look at how the piriformis helps with hip stability.

Typically, when we consider the action of the piriformis, we immediately think of lateral rotation. And that is absolutely correct. Envision a nice walk along the beach. You are strolling along, with your hips moving between hip flexion and extension. When you move into hip flexion, the piriformis assists in abducting the hip. hip. This abduction of the femur is critical, as it allows you to shift your body weight to the opposite side. This is pretty important; without this shift, you would be imbalanced and likely fall.

Now, about that sciatic nerve. Have you ever used the expression “pain in the butt”? Well, an injury to the piriformis muscle truly is a pain in the butt. Imagine that the piriformis is swollen or has a contracture. This altered configuration of the muscle may impinge the sciatic nerve, resulting in pain in the buttocks, pain with hip movements such as active lateral rotation or passive hip flexion, numbness and/or tingling down the posterior lower extremity, and pain when sitting for extended periods of time—like attending class or driving your car on a long road trip.

If you recall, in Chapter 4, we discussed true sciatica with pain originating from the lumbar spine. The piriformis impingement of the sciatic nerve is referred to as pseudo-sciatica,  piriformis syndrome, or deep gluteal syndrome. But just because it is not “true” does not mean the pain is not real. A hint to differentiate between the two conditions is that pain with true sciatica originates in the lumbar region (low back) (6.18), and pain stemming from pseudo-sciatica originates in the buttocks (6.19).

The Adductor Group

The adductor muscle group is located on the medial aspect of the hip and thigh (6.20, 6.21), and just as the name indicates, they contribute to adduction of the thigh. But a few of them do more than adduction, so it is important for a clinician to understand the distinction. There are five adductors in this group.

 

6.20 Anterior view of the adductor group

6.21 Medial view of the adductor group

 

  • The adductor longus is the primary adductor of the thigh. As its name suggests, it is the longest of the three muscles with adductor in their name and truly the only one of these adductors that is palpable, as it has a more anterior position than the deeper adductor brevis and magnus.

  • The adductor brevis is deep to the adductor longus and the pectineus and is the smallest muscle in the group. In collaboration with the pectineus, it also contributes to hip flexion.

  • The adductor magnus is the largest (so aptly named!) and most posterior of the adductor muscles. The adductor magnus is unique in that it contributes not only to adduction of the thigh but to both flexion and extension of the thigh as well. How is that possible? It is a result of location—the locations of its origins and insertions, which ultimately determine its functions. The part of the adductor magnus that allows for adduction and flexion of the hip originates from the inferior pubic ramus and inserts onto the linea aspera and the medial supracondylar ridge of the femur. The part of the adductor magnus that contributes to hip extension and adduction originates from the ischial tuberosity and inserts on the adductor tubercle of the femur.

  • The gracilis is perhaps the most notable muscle of the adductor group. It is the most superficial and the longest of the group and, thus, the easiest to palpate. However, it is the weakest of the adductors. Similar to the TFL, it also crosses both the hip and knee complexes, contributing to movement in each area—hip/thigh adduction as well as knee flexion and medial (internal) rotation of the knee joint (tibiofemoral joint).

  • The pectineus is unique in that it sits between the anterior and medial aspect of the hip/thigh. Because of its anatomical location, it is innervated by two nerves: the femoral (anterior aspect) and obturator (medial aspect). It also shares its role as part of the floor of the femoral triangle with the adductor longus. An injury to the pectineus may be misdiagnosed as an injury to the gracilis because of their close proximity to one another. However, the actions of the two muscles differ, and that is how a clinician can determine which muscle is injured. The pectineus contributes to hip flexion, adduction, and medial (internal) rotation of the hip; as noted above, the gracilis contributes to hip/thigh adduction, knee flexion, and medial rotation of the knee joint. A patient with a pectineus injury will likely exhibit pain and/or dysfunction with both hip flexion and adduction.

Clinical Relevance of the Acetabulofemoral Joint

Let us consider again the football player who experienced the posterior dislocation. Although anterior dislocations can occur, most hip dislocations—approximately 90%—are posterior.3 They are caused by a combination of forced movements: hip flexion, adduction, and medial (internal) rotation, often with an axial load. These loads force the femoral head to move in a posterior direction and away from the acetabulum (6.22), making the injured leg appear shorter.

 

6.22 Posterior hip dislocation

 

A common hip injury is a labral tear (6.23), which often occurs as a secondary injury from femoroacetabular impingement (FAI), trauma (e.g., dislocations), dysplasia, capsular laxity, and degeneration. Although a tear to the labrum did not happen in the scenario with the football player, the trauma of the posterior dislocation could have resulted in one on the posterior labrum. (In fact, the first time a labral tear was diagnosed was in 1957, secondary to a posterior dislocation.)

 

6.23 Intact acetabular labrum (left, highlighted in blue) versus labral tear (right, highlighted in red)

 

Trauma to the hip can also cause injury to the ligaments that support the acetabulofemoral joint or, less likely, damage to the sciatic nerve. This football player was very fortunate.
