Bursae within the Knee Complex

Functional Anatomy

If you recall, a bursa is a fluid-filled sac that sits between soft tissue and bone to reduce friction. Given the vast number of tendons that are located in and around the knee complex, it is easy to understand that there are also vast numbers of bursae. In this section, we will look at several of these bursae (7.50, 7.51, 7.52).

By reducing friction between tissue and bone, bursae prevent excessive wear and tear on the respective soft tissue. For example, due to the pes anserine bursa, the pes anserine tendon does not experience excessive wear as it slides over the lateral epicondyle of the femur.

Clinical Relevance of the Bursae

An acute or chronic inflammatory condition may develop within a bursa due to trauma, infection, overuse, and hemorrhage. The bursae in the knee complex are very susceptible to acute and chronic injuries. With an acute or chronic bursitis, an individual may experience swelling, limited range of motion, redness, warmth, and pain in and around the knee complex.

Some notable injuries to the bursae are Baker’s cyst (popliteal cyst) (7.53), housemaid’s or carpet-layer’s knee (prepatellar bursitis) (7.54), and clergyman’s knee (infrapatellar bursitis) (7.55, 7.56).
