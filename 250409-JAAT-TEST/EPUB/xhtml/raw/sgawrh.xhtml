Linking It All Together

Just as the human body is linked together by the kinetic chain, let us link what we learned in Chapter 4. A good way to do that is to revisit the questions from the beginning of the chapter to inspire discussion with your peers and instructor. We can also link to current evidence to further explore the questions.

To link to generalized discussion points online, go to booksofdiscovery.com, “For Students.”

  • Are you able to identify the anatomical structures that are involved when a golfer suffers from a herniated disc? How can an erratic golf swing lead to a herniated disc?
    • While there many possible answers to this question, one possible explanation may be:
      • If a golf swing is technically flawed, there will be a greater risk of injury to the low back due to greater rotational and compressive forces acting on the low back, particularly at the L-4–L-5 intervertebral space.
    • To link to an example of current evidence to support a discussion, go to:

Nathan Edwards, Clark Dickin, and Henry Wang, “Low Back Pain and Golf: A Review of Biomechanical Risk Factors,” Sports Medicine and Health Science 2, no. 1 (March 2020): 10–18, https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9219256/.

  • Forward head posture is common these days due to our excessive use of handheld phones and computers. Which vertebral region is altered and why is a forward head posture problematic?
    • A forward head posture may change the shape of the thorax which may be detrimental for respiration. Additionally, it may have an impact on the abdominal muscles, such that they become shortened. This shortening of the abdominal muscles limits the mobility of the thorax and negatively affects respiration.
    • To link to examples of current evidence to support a discussion, go to:

Taiichi Koseki et al., “Effect of Forward Head Posture on Thoracic Shape and Respiratory Function,” Journal of Physical Therapy Science 31, no. 1 (January 2019): 63–68, https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6348172/.

and

Michael Streifer et al., “The Potential Role of the Cervical Spine in Sports-Related Concussion: Clinical Perspectives and Considerations for Risk Reduction,” Journal of Orthopaedic and Sports Physical Therapy 49, no. 3 (March 2019): 202–08, https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7102493/.

  • What is a hunchback (kyphosis) position? Can you identify the vertebral region where this may occur?
    • The two most common causes of kyphosis or hyperkyphosis relate to the aging process.
      • In most individuals, the muscles become weaker with aging. The decrease in muscle strength makes it difficult to maintain the appropriate thoracic posture.
      • The second cause of a change in the natural kyphotic curvature of the thoracic spine is degenerative changes of the spine.
    • A third and less common cause of kyphosis is vertebral fractures.
    • To link to an example of current evidence to support a discussion, go to:

M. C. Koelé, W. F. Lems, and H. C. Willems, “The Clinical Relevance of Hyperkyphosis: A Narrative Review,” Frontiers in Endocrinology (Lausanne) 11 (2020): 5, https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6993454/.
