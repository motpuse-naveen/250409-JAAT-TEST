Vertebrae

As you may recall, the spine is designated by its different vertebral regions: cervical, thoracic, lumbar, sacral, and coccyx. It has four natural curvatures (4.4).

4.4 Natural curvatures of the spine

 

You may also recall that the spine consists of 33 vertebrae and each region of the spine has a particular number of vertebrae: seven cervical vertebrae, twelve thoracic vertebrae, five lumbar vertebrae, five fused sacral vertebrae, and four fused coccyx vertebrae (4.5). Each vertebral region is designed to facilitate either mobility or stability.

4.5 Vertebral column

 

Cervical Vertebrae

The cervical vertebrae are smaller than the thoracic and lumbar vertebrae, both in the bodies and the spinous processes (4.6, 4.7, 4.8). With their small sizes, the cervical vertebrae are designed to enable a great deal of motion at the neck including flexion, extension, rotation, and lateral flexion.

4.8 Cervical vertebra

 

Their spinous processes are bifurcated (split or fork-shaped) at the ends and also have a unique slope to them. While C-1 (atlas) does not truly have a spinous process, the remaining cervical spinous processes have an increasing downward slope from C-2 to- C-7. The cervical spine has a natural lordotic curvature (4.9).

 

4.9 A 3D view of the cervical vertebrae

 

Over time, the lordotic curvature may become exaggerated due to poor habitual neck posture. As a result, there is hyperextension of the upper cervical vertebrae and forward translation of the cervical vertebrae. This is commonly referred to as forward head posture (FHP) (4.10). FHP tends to increase the stress on the cervical vertebrae and its supporting structures and limit cervical range of motion. Take a moment to consider what may contribute to FHP. As you may have guessed, one factor is our obsession with media devices such as laptops and smartphones (4.11).

Thoracic Vertebrae

The thoracic vertebrae bodies and spinous processes are larger than those of the cervical vertebrae but smaller than those of the lumbar vertebrae (4.12). Each of the vertebrae serves as an attachment for the ribs. The thoracic spine has a natural kyphotic curvature (4.13).

4.12 Thoracic vertebra

 

4.13 A 3D view of the thoracic vertebrae

 

Because of their structural arrangement, the thoracic vertebrae are the least mobile. They have very limited flexion and extension due to the close proximity between the spinous processes and ribs. The most flexible movement of the thoracic spine is the rotation that occurs within the area of T-5 to T-8, but even that is quite minimal.

The intervertebral disc height in this region is less than that in the other vertebral regions. The thoracic spinous processes also continue the increasing downward slope seen among the C-2 to C-7 spinous processes.

Lumbar Vertebrae

The lumbar vertebrae are the workhorses of the spine, with massive bodies compared to those of other regions. The bodies of the lumbar vertebrae are thicker and wider, and thus, stronger than other vertebral bodies. Their spinous processes are also thicker, but they are much shorter, making them quite dense (4.14). Similar to the cervical spine, the lumbar spine has a natural lordotic curvature (4.15).

4.14 Lumbar vertebra

 

4.15 A 3D view of the lumbar vertebrae

 

The thick, dense makeup of the lumbar vertebrae affords these vertebrae the ability to bear incredible weight, making this region the prominent weight-bearing section of the spine. Despite its important role to absorb and transmit the forces acting upon it, the lumbar region also has some propensity for movement—not as much as the cervical spine but more than that of the thoracic region. The lumbar region allows for flexion, extension, rotation, and side bending.

 


 

What are some activities that place extra weight-bearing loads on the lumbar vertebrae?

  • Performing good-morning squats
  • Walking up and down stairs
  • Carrying a child on your shoulders

 

Sacrum

As previously noted, the sacrum consists of five fused vertebrae, leaving no spaces nor need for intervertebral discs (4.16). It has a natural kyphotic curvature (4.17).

4.16 Sacrum

 

4.17 A 3D view of the sacrum

 

The sacrum has an important role in the human kinetic chain, connecting the spine to the pelvis. The position of the sacrum plays a critical part in aligning the spine over the pelvis, giving us our upright posture. As long as we maintain this upright position and the normal curvature of our vertebral column, there will be little strain on our muscles and supporting structures of the spine and thorax. If we deviate from that upright position—due to postural distortions resulting from muscle weakness or irregular curvature of the spine (e.g., kyphosis, lordosis, scoliosis)—we may be susceptible to muscle strains or herniated discs.

Coccyx

The coccyx region of the spine consists of four fused vertebrae, resembling a solid structure (4.18). However, it may contain anywhere between three and five fused vertebrae. It rounds out the spine, as it is the distal end, and it has a natural kyphotic curvature (4.19). The proximal coccyx and distal sacrum are connected via a fibrocartilaginous joint.

 

4.18 Coccyx

 

4.19 A 3D view of the coccyx

 

The coccyx plays an important role in weight-bearing of the body, particularly in the seated position. In conjunction with the ischial spines of the pelvis, it provides support, as the coccyx and ischial spines form a tripod position (4.20).
