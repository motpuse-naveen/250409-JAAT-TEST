Joints of the Leg and Foot

To accompany Trail Guide to the Body, Chapter 7, Leg & Foot

Imagine two point guards on the same basketball team, in their first game of the season. At the end of the first half, the starting point guard dribbles from one end of the court to the other on a fast break. As she stops, pivots, and rotates on her right knee to make a bounce pass, she collapses to the ground, clutching her knee in pain. While the athletic trainer is evaluating the injury in the athletic training facility, the second half of the game begins. Within the initial minutes, the backup point guard also collapses on the court—having suffered the exact same mechanism of injury as the starting point guard! The result of both injury evaluations: each point guard has torn her anterior cruciate ligament (ACL).

What are the odds of two players tearing their ACLs in the same game? An unlucky coincidence to be sure—but also a true story. The story has a happy ending: both players came back to play phenomenally the next season. A major contributing factor was the role the athletic trainer played in their rehabilitation. Although both players suffered the same injury, the athletic trainer developed individualized rehabilitation programs for each. Using specialized plans over a cookie-cutter approach ensured that each player experienced maximum benefits.

For athletes and nonathletes alike, muscle and ligament injuries in the knee, ankle, and foot can have big impacts on mobility and affect the body’s entire kinetic chain.

The lower extremity is one of the most commonly injured areas of the body—particularly in individuals who participate in sports or who are physically active. For example:

  • Injury to the patellofemoral joint is one of the most frequent injuries to the knee complex. While an ACL injury captures most of the headlines, it does so because it is an acute injury of the knee complex and can be quite debilitating, often requiring surgery—it is not actually the most common injury to the knee.

  • A lateral ankle sprain is a common leg injury and can be problematic because it often leads to chronic ankle instability.

  • No matter the injury to the foot—acute or chronic, level of degree, or the location—all types have the potential to negatively impact the kinetic chain and, ultimately, normal daily function.

As a future clinician, you will want an in-depth understanding of the anatomy of the knee, ankle, and foot to better recognize the structures involved in these and a variety of other injuries.
