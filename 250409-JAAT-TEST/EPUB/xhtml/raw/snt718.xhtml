It is a hot, humid day for an intercollegiate preseason football scrimmage. The starting running back is expected to play only the first half of the scrimmage to minimize his risk of injury. The team really needs him to be healthy for the full season, as they expect to win their conference.

Just before halftime, the running back is running along the sideline, destined for the end zone. However, a defensive end from the opposing team has an alternative plan and dives at the legs of the running back. As he makes the tackle, the defensive back forces the running back’s right leg into flexion, medial (internal) rotation, and adduction—the classic mechanism of injury for a posterior dislocation of the hip.

You may be thinking that this injury would surely keep him out for the season. But thanks to the quick-thinking sports medicine team, their orthopedic surgeon performed the Bigelow Maneuver and successfully reduced the posterior dislocation. The time lapse between the injury and reduction made for a better outcome and full recovery. A healthy hip and pelvis are essential for performing daily functional activities. The variety of injuries that can occur in the pelvis and hip—whether acute or chronic—can pose a diagnostic dilemma. It is critically important to recognize and manage these injuries because of their potential long-term impact.

Compared to sports-related injuries in all other areas of the body, those of the hip and pelvis make up a smaller percentage (5%–6%) in physically active adults. However, the clinician’s ability to accurately diagnose and rehabilitate them is essential. If not properly identified and managed, a hip and pelvis pathology can have devastating consequences on physical activity—short and long term—as well as negative implications across the kinetic chain (6.1). For example, if the hip and pelvis are injured, adjustments in gait are likely to occur, which may lead to a lower extremity injury or a back injury.
