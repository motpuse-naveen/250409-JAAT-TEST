The Lisfranc Joint

Functional Anatomy

The Lisfranc (tarsometatarsal) joint connects the metatarsals and tarsal bones. Specifically, it connects all five metatarsals with the cuneiforms and cuboid (7.91).

7.91 The Lisfranc joint

 

Having minimal motion, the Lisfranc joint is essential for stabilization of the proximal transverse arch during the toe-off/push-off phase of walking. It also transfers forces from the lower leg to the forefoot while walking.

Supporting Connective Tissue

The Lisfranc ligament is a wide band supporting the Lisfranc joint. The transverse ligaments connect the bases of the four lateral metatarsals. However, no transverse ligament connects the first metatarsal to the second metatarsal. With limited support within the articulating surfaces of the first and second cuneiforms to the first and second metatarsals, this joint is considered the weak link of the midfoot and is susceptible to injury.

This area also is considered the keystone, as it supports the entire tarsometatarsal joint; it is the true Lisfranc joint (7.92).

7.92 The true Lisfranc joint

 

Supporting Soft Tissue

The Lisfranc joint is a complex area in the midfoot, relying on the skeletal structure as well as soft tissue for joint stability. The soft tissues act upon both the dorsal and plantar aspects of this complex. The muscular support of this complex is provided by the tibialis anterior (7.93), tibialis posterior (7.94), and the fibularis (peroneus) longus (7.95).

Clinical Relevance of the Lisfranc Joint

Low-energy types of force are common causes of a Lisfranc sprain or dislocation (7.96, 7.97). This type of injury is often seen in soccer and basketball players. It can also be found in occupations such as landscaping, roofing, and flooring installation.

The Medial Longitudinal Arch

Functional Anatomy

You may recall that the medial longitudinal arch (MLA) is made up of the calcaneus, talus, navicular, three cuneiforms, and the first, second, and third metatarsals. The navicular is the center of the MLA—the heart and “sole” of the foot (7.98, 7.99).

 

7.98 The medial longitudinal arch (MLA)

 

7.99 Arches of the foot

 

The medial longitudinal arch plays a critical role in the foot while you are walking. It helps dissipate forces acting upon the foot, as it is a shock absorber. Additionally, the MLA assists in propelling your foot forward. Interestingly, the position of the navicular within the MLA determines whether you have a normal foot, a pes cavus foot (high MLA arch), or a pes planus foot (flat foot) (7.100).

 

7.100 Arch type and foot alignment

 

If the navicular tuberosity is in a neutral position or in line with the distal pole of the medial malleolus and the first metatarsophalangeal joint, that constitutes a normal MLA. If the navicular tuberosity sits above that imaginary line—the Feiss line (7.101)—that is a pes cavus foot. If the navicular tuberosity sits below the imaginary line, the result is a pes planus foot (flat MLA arch or flat foot). A pes planus foot is often associated with individuals who overpronate when walking and running.

 

7.101 The Feiss line

Supporting Connective Tissue

What determines the position of the navicular within the medial longitudinal arch? The supporting tissues play a big role. The primary support for the arch is the plantar calcaneonavicular ligament, also called the spring ligament (7.102).

 

7.102 The plantar calcaneonavicular ligament, or spring ligament

 

This ligament supports the weakest joint within the medial longitudinal arch, the articulation between the talus and navicular. The spring ligament is essential for the stability of the MLA, as it is very elastic and is able to rebound from overpressure applied to the MLA. Muscle weakness in the tibialis posterior, tibialis anterior, and fibularis (peroneus) longus may also contribute to a change in the position of the navicular within the MLA. Laxity in the deltoid ligament or plantar aponeurosis (fascia) (7.103) may also affect the position of the navicular.

 

7.103 Plantar aponeurosis (fascia)

 

Clinical Relevance of the Medial Longitudinal Arch

If any of the structures we have just discussed are injured, it may have a temporary or long-term impact on the medial longitudinal arch. For example, if there is consistent overpressure on the plantar calcaneonavicular ligament, it may become lax and lose its ability to spring back to its normal position. This would leave you with a pes planus foot—and an inability to set records in the high jump (7.104).

 

7.104 A healthy MLA may result in jumping to new heights!

 

 

Can you think of any injuries or medical conditions that may lead to a pes planus foot?

Some examples:

  • Obesity

  • Weak tibialis posterior

  • Overpronation of the foot

  • Fallen medial longitudinal arch

 

 

This is a good time to revisit the importance of dorsiflexion in regard to the human kinetic chain because it has many implications. As you may recall, the plantar aponeurosis (fascia) extends from the base of the calcaneus all the way to your toes. Also, in younger people (7.105), the distal attachment of the Achilles tendon connects with the plantar aponeurosis.

 

7.105 Younger feet have more connection.

 

Why is this important? If there is muscle tightness in the triceps surae or adhesions in the plantar aponeurosis, you are likely to have limited dorsiflexion and, potentially, toe extension. The human kinetic chain really does begin at the toes.
