The Atlantooccipital and Atlantoaxial Joints

In the proximal end of the cervical spine are two articulations, the atlantooccipital joint and the atlantoaxial joint (4.21).

 

4.21 The atlantooccipital and atlantoaxial joints

 

Through the range of motion (neck flexion, extension, and rotation) available at these joints, we are able to adjust our head position to gain a better view of the golf ball after we have teed off, to better hear our favorite teacher, or to nonverbally communicate with others. The atlantooccipital joint contributes to 50% of flexion and extension of the neck: the “yes” nonverbal response. The atlantoaxial joint contributes to 50% of all cervical rotation: the “no” nonverbal response.1

Intervertebral Discs

As you can imagine, a great deal of stress is placed upon the vertebrae, particularly in the lumbar region. The vertebral bodies need a buffer to protect them from the wear and tear of the compressive and tensile forces acting upon them. Within the cervical, thoracic, and lumbar vertebrae regions, such a buffer exists: the intervertebral discs.

Functional Anatomy

Within the vertebral column are a total of 23 intervertebral discs: six cervical (there is no disc between C-1 and C-2), twelve thoracic, and five lumbar (4.22). Collectively, these discs constitute 25% to 33% of the length of the spine.2

 

4.22 Intervertebral discs in the lumbar region

 

These discs play an incredibly important role in the overall health of our spine. They provide stability while affording flexibility of the spine. Arguably, their most important role within the spine is that of a shock absorber. The ability of the disc to dissipate compressive forces—reducing risk of injury and/or degenerative changes—allows us to have a greater quality of life.

At a quick glance, the intervertebral discs may look quite simplistic. But do not let that fool you. Each disc is made up of three components, each with a critical role (4.23):

  1. Nucleus pulposus (NP)
    • The nucleus pulposus is the gel-like center of the intervertebral disc. It is composed of approximately 66% to 86% water,3 and the remainder is type II collagen.
    • The NP contributes to both the stability and flexibility of the spine through minimizing the compressive forces acting upon the spine.
  2. Annulus fibrosus (AF)
    • This fibrous connective tissue encapsulates the NP. The annulus fibrosus is a ring-shaped disc and is what gives the intervertebral disc its shape. It, too, consists of collagen fibers: type I and type II. Type I collagen is found in the outer AF; type II collagen is more prominent within the inner NP. That makes sense—the closer the AF is to the NP, the more type I collagen fibers it has.
    • Ensuring the NP stays in the center of the intervertebral disc is not the only role for the AF. It also transmits the forces stemming from rotation, flexion, and extension of the spine.
  3. Cartilaginous endplates
    • These connect the discs to adjacent vertebrae, ensuring the discs remain in place and attached to their corresponding vertebrae.

 

4.23 Intervertebral disc

 

Let us take a moment to simplify the NP and AF. Picture, if you will, the humble jelly doughnut.

Now compare the structure of the jelly doughnut to the intervertebral disc. The jelly of the doughnut is what the nucleus pulposus is to the intervertebral disc; it is the gooey center. The dough portion of the jelly doughnut is the annulus fibrosus. It is what keeps the jelly, or the NP, in place. If the dough, or AF, tears—as when you bite into the doughnut—the jelly of NP will leak out beyond its border. That is perfectly fine for a jelly doughnut, but not for our intervertebral discs.

Clinical Relevance of the Intervertebral Discs

When talking with a group of students, I discovered how they envisioned a herniated disc prior to learning about it; they thought the disc slid across the vertebrae, moving from its original position. Although that surprised me at first, this thought process made sense if they had not yet studied the anatomy of an intervertebral disc.

A herniated disc occurs when the nucleus pulposus extends beyond the border of the vertebrae (remember that jelly leaking out of the doughnut). The disc itself does not move, as it is anchored to the adjacent vertebrae via the cartilaginous endplates. The annulus fibrosus has either been stretched or torn, allowing the NP to creep beyond its vertebral body (4.24).

 


4.24 Herniated disc

 

This condition can be quite problematic, as you might expect. The intervertebral disc allows for space between the adjacent vertebrae as well as the nerve roots located within the intervertebral space. As the NP creeps beyond its border, it pushes the AF even farther, increasing the probability of the AF impinging upon a nerve root of the inferior vertebrae. The most common intervertebral spaces for a disc herniation are between L-5 and S-1. A herniated disc typically has signs and symptoms including a sharp or burning pain that radiates along the path of the compressed nerve root, numbness and tingling, and decreased sensation along the path of the nerve root.

Most disc herniations are likely due to an injury resulting from lifting or twisting. Thinking back to the opening story of the chapter, that twisting mechanism of injury is what led to the golfer’s herniated disc. Working on his swing not only improved his golf game, it also helped him keep his intervertebral discs healthy (4.25).

Let us also reflect back on the tree analogy. The spine supports the body’s physical structure just as the trunk of a tree supports its physical structure. Both the spine and the tree have roots. Throughout the vertebral column, 31 pairs of roots and their associated spinal nerves innervate the periphery of the body, enabling movement and sensation. Within the tree, the roots provide water and nutrients and serve as an anchor for the tree.

The spine and tree also have similar structures that provide protection. In the human body, the thorax acts much like a cage to safeguard the spine and internal organs. The bark of a tree protects the trunk from damage and harm.

So the next time you are out for a walk, pause and marvel at these characteristics of a tree—knowing our own bodies have a similar beauty and strength (4.26).
