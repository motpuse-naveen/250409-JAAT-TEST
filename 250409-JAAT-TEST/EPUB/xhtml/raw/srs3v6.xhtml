The Knee Complex

When most people think of the knee, they are probably thinking of the tibiofemoral joint. This articulation is one of three in the knee complex: the tibiofemoral joint, the patellofemoral joint, and the tibiofibular joint. While the tibiofibular joint is important and may be injured, most injuries in the knee complex occur within the other two articulations. So this section will focus on the tibiofemoral and patellofemoral joints, along with their supporting ligaments.

The Tibiofemoral Joint

Functional Anatomy

The bony anatomy of the tibiofemoral joint (the true knee joint) consists of the tibial condyles (concave structures) and the femoral condyles (convex structures) (7.2, 7.3). What about the fibula? The fibula is a component of the knee complex but is not part of the tibiofemoral joint because it connects only to the tibia, not to the femur.

7.3 A 3D view of the tibiofemoral joint

 

The tibiofemoral joint is a true synovial hinge joint that moves easily from full knee extension into full knee flexion, barring any structural or soft tissue limitations (7.4). Joint mobility is important for daily living activities (standing, walking, sitting) as well as for athletic ones (kicking, jumping, squatting, swimming) (7.5). The tibiofemoral joint is a critical link in the overall kinetic chain of the human body and plays an important role in posture, stance, gait, and balance. Without healthy bilateral tibiofemoral joints, we are destined to suffer imbalances throughout the kinetic chain.

7.5 Tibiofemoral joint motion while running

 

Think back to one of the injured basketball players at the beginning of this chapter and imagine that you are walking out on the basketball court to treat her. As you walk, your legs alternate being in the closed-packed position of full extension (7.6). In this full weight-bearing position, your tibiofemoral joint has maximum stability. Why? Because your tibial plateau and femoral condyles are at their highest level of congruency (maximum surface contact). However, the maximum stability gained equals the least amount of mobility. Try walking with both your knees in full extension!

 

7.6 This athletic trainer (right) extends his left knee, demonstrating the closed-packed position. The player (left) flexes her injured right knee, demonstrating the open-packed position.

 

 

When is the knee in a closed-packed position?

Some examples:

  • Stance leg of walking

  • End stage of a power clean

  • The end position when performing short-arc quad sets

 

 

The basketball player assumes a position in the injured leg that is only partially weight-bearing and has a slight knee bend (7.6). By changing this knee position, she has reduced the congruency in the tibiofemoral joint. It is now in an open-packed (or loose-packed) position and there is less congruence (and less stability) between the femoral and tibial condyles. If you look closely at her knee, you will note that it is between 20° and 30° of flexion.

 

 

When is the knee in an open (loose)-packed position?

Some examples:

  • Swing leg of walking

  • Accommodating a knee with swelling

  • The start position when performing short-arc quad sets
