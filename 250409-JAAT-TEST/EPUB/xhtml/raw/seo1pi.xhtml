Bursae within the Hip Complex and Pelvis

Functional Anatomy

The hip complex and pelvis contain several bursae. Here we will focus on the three most clinically relevant: ischiogluteal, trochanteric, and iliopsoas. (6.37, 6.38, 6.39).

With all the varied motions occurring at the acetabulofemoral joint, there are soft tissue structures that cross over bony prominences. These soft tissues need to be protected from the constant wear and tear on the hip. Thankfully, the bursae within the hip complex and pelvis handle that job, keeping our hip healthy and mobile.

  • The ischiogluteal (or iliopectineal) bursa acts as a buffer between the gluteus maximus and surface of the ischial tuberosity.

  • The trochanteric bursa, also known as the greater trochanteric bursa or the subgluteus maximus bursa, is located deep to the gluteus maximus muscle and cushions the tensor fasciae latae and iliotibial band from the greater trochanter.

  • What is the largest bursa in the human body? It is the iliopsoas bursa, which is positioned over the lesser trochanter and protects the iliopsoas tendon from undue compression and friction.

Clinical Relevance of the Bursae

One example of bursitis in this region is trochanteric bursitis, also known as greater trochanteric bursitis, which may be caused by direct trauma or repetitive microtrauma (6.40). An example of direct trauma is a volleyball player who dives to save a ball and lands directly on their hip, compressing the trochanteric bursa. It becomes very inflamed with pain over the area. A distance runner may experience repetitive trauma when the tensor fasciae latae and/or iliotibial band consistently rubs over the bursa, resulting in compression and friction. Eventually, the bursa becomes inflamed and a nagging pain in the area is present. Either way, normal activity such as walking, running, and sitting may be impacted until the inflammation subsides and the injury is properly managed.
