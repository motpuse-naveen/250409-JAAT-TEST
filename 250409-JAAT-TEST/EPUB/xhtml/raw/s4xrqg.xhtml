The Distal Tibiofibular Joint

Functional Anatomy

You may recall the skeletal structures of the distal tibiofibular joint—the distal tibia and fibula (7.76).

7.76 The distal tibiofibular joint

 

This articulation may seem simple, but it is critically important to the normal functioning of the kinetic chain. It coordinates with the talocrural and subtalar joints for the multiplanar movement of the rearfoot—supination and pronation. It also plays a significant role transmitting forces from the leg to the foot, particularly when weight-bearing. When injured, one could say that is the “bearer” of bad news!

Supporting Connective Tissue

The distal tibiofibular joint is supported by three ligaments: the distal anterior tibiofibular ligament, the distal posterior tibiofibular ligament, and the crural interosseous ligament (7.77, 7.78, 7.79).

 

7.77 Anterior view of ligaments supporting the distal tibiofibular joint

 

7.78 Posterior view of ligaments supporting the distal tibiofibular joint

 

7.79 Lateral view of ligaments supporting the distal tibiofibular joint

 

The distal anterior tibiofibular ligament and the distal posterior tibiofibular ligament resist the transverse/horizontal forces acting upon the distal tibiofibular joint. The crural interosseous ligament is unique, as it is a thickening or extension of the interosseous membrane designed to prevent further spreading of the distal tibiofibular joint in a weight-bearing position. Like the interosseous membrane, the crural interosseous ligament runs at an oblique angle between the tibia and fibula. So it also protects against rotation and abduction—a common mechanism of injury for a syndesmosis sprain, which we will explore next.

Clinical Relevance of the Distal Tibiofibular Joint

Connective tissue injury to the distal tibiofibular joint is synonymous with a high ankle sprain, or a syndesmosis sprain (7.80, 7.81).

7.80 Syndesmosis (high ankle) sprain

 

7.81 Ligament damage from syndesmosis (high ankle) sprains

 

This is a more debilitating sprain than the much more common supination sprain. Why? When you take a step forward, or even when you are simply standing, the dome of the talus is applying force in an upward direction and placing stress on the distal tibiofibular joint.

Imagine it like this: Every time you step, the supporting tissues of the distal tibiofibular joint are working hard to prevent the tibia and fibula from splitting apart from one another. Say an NFL running back suffers a shoestring tackle, resulting in a high ankle sprain (7.82)—the talus is forced superiorly and causes stress on the supporting tissues of the distal tibiofibular joint.

 

7.82 A shoestring tackle can cause a high ankle sprain.

 

So why is this injury more debilitating? The ankle will likely be non-weight-bearing in the initial stage of the rehabilitation process, and the return to play will likely take longer.
