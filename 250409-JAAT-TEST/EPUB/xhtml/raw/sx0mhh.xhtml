Clinical Assessment of Cranial Nerves

While we are on the topic of the cranial nerves, let us take a closer look at each one and how to assess if it is functioning properly.

CN I Olfactory

Time to stop and smell the roses (5.6). To assess CN I, the olfactory nerve, instruct the patient to close their eyes while you occlude (close up or cover) one nostril. Have the patient smell a familiar scent as you hold it less than 30 cm (about 12 inches) away from the unoccluded nostril. Repeat on the other side. Failure of the patient to properly identify the smell is a positive test for deficiency.

 

5.6 Smelling the roses is possible because of the olfactory nerve.

 

CN II Optic

Lights, camera, action! When assessing CN II, the optic nerve, we need to determine both normal visual acuity and the pupil’s reaction to light.

Visual acuity:

  • The clinician takes a position three feet away from the patient as the patient focuses on the clinician’s eyes or nose.
  • The clinician holds a select number of fingers up in a particular quadrant.
  • The clinician instructs the patient to identify how many fingers they see.
  • Each eye should be assessed separately.
  • As one eye is being assessed, the other eye should be covered (e.g., patient holds their hand over their uninvolved eye).
  • Inability to identify the appropriate number of fingers is a positive test for deficiency.
  • The clinician could also utilize a Snellen eye chart if one is available (5.7).

 

5.7 A Snellen eye chart can be used to test visual acuity.

 

Pupillary reflex to light:

  • Again, the clinician stands approximately three feet away from the patient as the patient focuses on the clinician's eyes or nose.
  • The clinician shines a penlight directly into the patient’s pupil for one to three seconds (5.8).
  • The clinician swiftly shifts the light to the other eye for one to three seconds, then shifts the light to the first eye.
  • The clinician closely observes the pupil for constriction throughout the test, noting any asymmetry between the pupils.
  • The clinician observes for both a direct response and a consensual response.
  • Any asymmetry is a positive sign for a deficiency with the optic nerve.

 

5.8 A clinician uses a penlight to test pupil reaction.

CN III Oculomotor, CN IV Trochlear, and CN VI Abducens

CN III is the oculomotor nerve, CN IV is the trochlear nerve, and CN VI is the abducens nerve. The clinician can assess all three of these motor nerves utilizing the same method. That makes it more efficient, which is particularly valuable in an emergency situation.

The H test/pattern is an effective assessment tool:

  • The clinician holds a pen or finger 30–40 cm (about 12–16 inches) directly in front of the patient.
  • The clinician instructs the patient to follow the pen/finger with only their eyes, ensuring they keep their head still.

If any or all of these cranial nerves are deficient, the clinician would note a deviation in the movement of the eye, an abnormal head posture, or nystagmus.

 

CN V Trigeminal

CN V, the trigeminal nerve, is numerically the first cranial nerve that is both sensory and motor.

To evaluate the sensory component of this cranial nerve:

  • The clinician lightly touches the patient’s face in various regions while their eyes are closed (5.9).

  • The clinician instructs the patient to indicate whether the sensation is dull/soft or sharp.

  • The clinician could also lightly brush the pointed tip of a reflex hammer and a cotton swab or ball to the patient's face to have them distinguish between sharp and dull/soft.

  • The inability of the patient to differentiate between dull/soft or sharp (or absence of any sensation) is indicative of a positive test.

 

5.9 A clinician lightly touches different regions of a patient’s face to assess the sensory component of the trigeminal nerve.

 

The motor component of the trigeminal nerve is assessed through the patient’s jaw strength.

  • The clinician instructs the patient to bite down on a tongue depressor (5.10).

  • The clinician then instructs them to not allow it to be pulled out as the clinician tugs on the tongue depressor.

  • If the patient cannot maintain a strong grip on the tongue depressor and hold it in place, that is considered a positive test.

  • Another method of assessment is asking the patient to open their mouth against resistance.

  • The clinician applies manual resistance while the patient attempts to open their mouth.

  • If their jaw deviates to the side, that is considered a positive sign likely due to a weakened pterygoid muscle (which is innervated by the trigeminal nerve).

  • Another helpful and easy assessment tool is to palpate the masseter muscles, noting any asymmetry, while the patient’s teeth are clenched.

 

5.10 Biting down on a tongue depressor aids in assessing jaw strength.

 

The muscles of mastication (chewing muscles) are innervated by the trigeminal.

It is not that uncommon in contact sports such as wrestling for participants to contract HSV-I. This is a highly infectious virus transmitted through oral-to-oral contact via saliva. The infection produces vesicles that most commonly form on or around the lips. However, vesicles may also appear along the hairline, forehead, and cheeks. The virus hibernates in the trigeminal nerve ganglia. This latent virus can produce an outbreak when an individual experiences stress, fatigue, exposure to sunlight, or a compromised immune system. An outbreak follows the dermatome pattern associated with the trigeminal nerve, which is why the vesicles form around the mouth, forehead, and cheeks.

Unfortunately, once you have HSV-I, you have it for a lifetime. It is one of the most widely spread viruses; an estimated 67% of the world population hosts HSV-I.1 While the virus is associated with cold sores, it can lead to severe pathologies such as encephalitis or keratoconjunctivitis. On very rare occasions, it also has the potential to impact the central nervous system. It is important for clinicians to educate patients with HSV-I about how easily it is spread and the potentially serious conditions beyond a simple cold sore.

 

CN VII Facial

With the exception of the muscles of mastication, all of the facial muscles are innervated by CN VII, the facial nerve. Also, remember that CN VII is both sensory and motor. Testing this cranial nerve is something you and a partner can have fun practicing!

To assess the sensory component, the clinician may assess the patient’s sense of taste.

  • Note that in an emergency situation, it is rare that taste would be assessed. For example, if the patient has an altered level of consciousness, the clinician would not want to place anything in their mouth.

  • In a non-emergency situation, if the clinician were to assess the patient’s sense of taste, they could implement the following strategy:

    • The clinician instructs the patient to close their eyes and stick out their tongue.

    • The clinician places a familiar taste on their tongue and asks them to identify it.

    • Failure of the patient to identify the taste would indicate a positive test.

    • Ideally, the clinician would want to utilize four types of taste: salty, bitter, sweet, and sour (5.11).

 

5.11 Sour taste! The facial nerve is at work here.

 

To assess the motor component:

  • The clinician simply asks the patient to smile, raise their eyebrows, puff out their cheeks, and blink twice (5.12).

  • Typically, these movements are done independent of one another. (Though you could also ask the patient to roll from one test right into the other.)

  • As the patient performs these motor tasks, the clinician observes for any inability to perform the actions or asymmetry from one side of the face to the other.

 

5.12 Puffing out the cheeks is one facial nerve test used to look for asymmetry.

 

CN VIII Vestibulocochlear

Can you listen to music and stand on one leg at the same time? Quite likely, yes (5.13). But if you have an injury to CN VIII, the vestibulocochlear nerve, you may not be able to do both—or either. Pathology of this nerve may be a result of direct trauma, congenital deformation, infection, vascular injury, or a tumor. Symptoms of a disruption of CN VIII may include tinnitus, nystagmus, vertigo, and/or hearing loss.

 

5.13 Typically, we can simultaneously listen and balance with a healthy vestibulocochlear nerve.

 

There are a variety of methods to assess both the auditory and vestibular (balance) components of CN VIII. Let us begin with examples for assessing the auditory component. One of the more common methods is to occlude (cover) one ear while assessing the other, and then repeat on the other side, noting any asymmetry. The clinician stands behind the patient and can perform either or both of the following methods to assess hearing:

  • Whisper three words and have the patient repeat them.

  • Snap your fingers and ask the patient how many times they heard the snap.

To assess balance, the clinician could implement a variety of activities. It is important to note that a gradual progression should be followed when performing these balance assessments. The clinician should closely monitor the patient during the testing to ensure they do not fall.

  • Standing position with two legs together; initially, with eyes open, and then with eyes closed

  • Standing position on one leg; initially, with eyes open, and then with eyes closed

  • Walking tandem: forward and backward

  • Standing in tandem; initially, with eyes open, and then with eyes closed

  • Romberg’s test (5.14); initially, with eyes open, and then with eyes closed

 

5.14 Romberg’s test assesses balance.

 

CN IX Glossopharyngeal and CN X Vagus

CN IX, the glossopharyngeal nerve, and CN X, the vagus nerve, can easily be assessed together and another way to be more effective in an emergency situation. The clinician instructs the patient to open their mouth and say “ah” (5.15). For evaluation purposes, the clinician notes whether the “ah” sound can be made by the patient. The clinician should also note whether the palate elevates symmetrically and the uvula remains in the midline. If weakness is present, the uvula will lift away from the paretic (partial paralysis) side of the palate. If the patient has difficulty with swallowing, it is likely the glossopharyngeal nerve is impaired. Additionally, if the patient has a strong voice when responding to your questions, it is likely the vagus nerve is intact.

 

5.15 Saying “ah!” is part of an effective test of the glossopharyngeal and vagus nerves.

 

CN XI Spinal Accessory

The focus of the assessment of CN XI, the spinal accessory nerve, is on the function of the sternocleidomastoid and trapezius muscles. As with many of the cranial nerves, there are a variety of ways to assess the integrity of these muscles:

To assess the left sternocleidomastoid:

  • The clinician places one hand on the patient’s right cheek.

  • The patient turns their head to the right while the clinician provides resistance.

    • Repeat the process on the opposite side.

  • The clinician provides resistance while the patient flexes to one side.

    • Repeat the process on the opposite side.

To assess the trapezius, the clinician should:

  • Inspect the shoulder and upper back for any asymmetry, drooping of the shoulder, or winging of the scapula.

    • To evaluate for winging of the scapula, instruct the patient to press their hands against a wall with their arms extended.

  • Note any atrophy of the trapezius or medial (internal) rotation of the humerus.

  • Note any hypertrophy or subluxation (partial dislocation) of the sternoclavicular joint due to increased strain on the joint from loss of trapezius muscle support.

  • Instruct the patient to shrug their shoulders while the clinician provides resistance by pressing down on the shoulders (5.16).

 

5.16 This patient is attempting to shrug their shoulders against the clinician’s resistance.

 

CN XII Hypoglossal

The simplest and most common way to assess for a deficient CN XII, the hypoglossal nerve, is to instruct the patient to stick out (protrude) their tongue (5.17). If the tongue deviates to the side, that is a positive test.

 

5.17 Protruding the tongue is one test for assessing the hypoglossal nerve.

 

Two additional tests can be utilized for improved assessment of the hypoglossal nerve:

  • Instruct the patient to press their tongue against each cheek while you provide gentle resistance to the cheek.

  • Instruct the patient to say words beginning with the letters T and D. If the patient suffers from hypoglossal palsy, they will find it difficult or be unable to pronounce the T and D.
