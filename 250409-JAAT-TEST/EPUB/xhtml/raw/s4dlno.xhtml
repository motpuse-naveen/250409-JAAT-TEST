The Menisci

Imagine walking around each day with your femoral condyles pushing down directly against the tibial plateau. The bones would soon begin to degenerate, causing great pain and triggering an inflammatory response. None of us wants to walk around in pain—we would quickly become sedentary. Thankfully, the bones of the tibiofemoral joint are protected by hyaline cartilage and two crescent-shaped fibrocartilages: the lateral and medial menisci.

Functional Anatomy

Both menisci have a limited blood supply and level of innervation. They also have limited ability to regenerate once injured or compromised. The thicker periphery of each meniscus has a somewhat better blood supply than their inner aspects, which gives these outside edges a slightly better regeneration rate than the medial aspects. The menisci are securely attached to the tibial plateau, and they are distinguishable from one another by their shape and location (7.7).

The menisci move with your tibia when you are walking, running, or riding a bicycle (7.8). As the concave surface of the tibia rolls along the femoral condyles, the menisci move in a posterior direction from full knee extension to full knee flexion. This protects the articulating surfaces of the tibiofemoral joint and prevents degeneration, helping us to keep moving throughout our lifespan.

 

7.8 A cyclist’s knees in motion

 

The composition of the medial meniscus—slightly thicker posteriorly than anteriorly—is particularly beneficial when in a fully flexed knee position, as when completing an Olympic lift such as the snatch (7.9).

 

7.9 The snatch lift incorporates a flexed knee position.

 

Functions of the menisci 

  • Reduce friction

  • Increase stability 

  • Provide joint lubrication

  • Provide shock absorption

  • Increase congruence and contact area

  • Resist shear, tension, and compression forces

  • Provide nutrition to and protect the hyaline cartilage

  • Provide body weight distribution and load transmission

  • Assist with proper arthrokinematics and proprioception

Another reason the menisci are incredibly important for daily functions is that they contain mechanoreceptors, located in the anterior and posterior horns (7.10). These mechanoreceptors include the Ruffini’s end organs, Pacinian corpuscles, and Golgi tendon organs (GTOs), which detect changes in deformation, pressure, tension, and range of motion in the joint, leading to neuromuscular inhibition when necessary. This ability to recognize and adapt to sensory changes within a joint allows the menisci to be key players for protecting us from ourselves.

 

7.10 The mechanoreceptors are located in the anterior and posterior horns of the menisci. 

 

Supporting Connective Tissue

It is easy to imagine that the menisci are floating between the femoral condyles and the tibial plateau. But if that were true, it would be impossible for the tibiofemoral joint to facilitate movement. Fortunately, the menisci are connected to the tibiofemoral joint via four ligaments: the transverse ligament, the ligament of Wrisberg, and the lateral and medial coronary ligaments (7.11, 7.12). These ligaments keep the menisci firmly in place and enable them to protect the articulating surfaces of the femur and tibia. The menisci play a critically important function in the health and stability of the tibiofemoral joint.

Clinical Relevance of the Menisci

The lateral meniscus is less likely to suffer an injury than the medial meniscus—a good thing, given its decreased vascularity compared to the medial meniscus. The medial meniscus experiences compressive forces (pushing surfaces together) and tensile forces (pulling surfaces apart) to a greater extent than the lateral meniscus does, due to the quadriceps angle , or Q angle (7.13), in both men and women.

 

7.13 The Q angle

 

Also, the medial meniscus is at an increased risk for injury, such as a tear (7.14), because of its relationship with the MCL and as a member of the Unhappy Triad (more about that later).

 

7.14 Meniscus tear

 

If you are involved in a sport or activity that involves compressive and/or tensile forces on the menisci—such as a catcher’s squat position (7.15)—you may experience damage to one or both menisci. Injury may come without the pain or swelling commonly associated with other connective tissue damage. If you have pain, it will likely be when your knee is fully flexed or fully extended, due to increased pressure on the more densely innervated area of the menisci. You may even feel a locking sensation and some instability of the tibiofemoral joint but have minimal or no swelling and/or pain if the ligaments connecting the menisci to the tibial plateau are not injured as well.

 

7.15 When a catcher is in a deep squat, the menisci are compressed.

 

 

 

What kinds of activities cause compressive and/or tensile forces that impact the menisci?

Some examples:

  • A gymnast completing a floor routine.

  • A valgus force applied to a flexed knee and a planted foot with the femur internally rotated may cause a tear of the medial meniscus.

  • A varus force applied to a flexed knee and a planted foot with the femur externally rotated may lead to a tear of the lateral meniscus.
